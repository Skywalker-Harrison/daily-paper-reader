Abstract 

Long document understanding has become increasingly crucial in natural lan-1

guage processing, with retrieval-based methods emerging as a promising solu-2

tion to address the context length limitations of large language models (LLMs). 3

However, existing approaches either treat documents as flat sequences or employ 4

arbitrary chunking strategies, failing to capture the inherent discourse structure 5

that guides human comprehension. We present DISRetrieval, a novel hierarchical 6

retrieval framework that leverages linguistic discourse structure to enhance long 7

document understanding. Our approach introduces three key innovations: (1) a 8

discourse-aware document organization framework that utilizes rhetorical structure 9

theory (RST) to create sentence-level hierarchical representations, preserving both 10 

semantic relationships and natural document flow; (2) an LLM-enhanced node 11 

representation technique that combines discourse structure with adaptive summa-12 

rization to enrich tree nodes with contextual information; and (3) a hierarchical 13 

evidence retrieval mechanism that effectively selects relevant content while main-14 

taining discourse coherence. Through comprehensive experiments on QASPER 15 

and QuALITY datasets, DISRetrieval demonstrates substantial improvements over 16 

existing methods in both token-level retrieval metrics and downstream question 17 

answering tasks. Our ablation studies confirm that incorporating discourse structure 18 

significantly enhances retrieval effectiveness across different document lengths 19 

and query types, validating the importance of linguistically-informed document 20 

representation in long-text understanding. 21 

1 Introduction 22 

Comprehending natural language documents is a fundamental task in natural language understanding 23 

[32 ]. With the increasing granularity and complexity of textual data, long document understanding 24 

has gained significant attention [ 3, 18 ]. Large language models (LLMs) have shown remarkable 25 

effectiveness for this task [ 8]. However, they still struggle with the challenges of memory constraints 26 

and restricted context lengths [ 4, 28 ]. Retrieval-based methods are a promising solution to these 27 

challenges [ 22 , 15 ]. These methods first split long documents into small pieces and then use a retrieval 28 

model to select the most relevant evidence, thereby easing the burden of long-context reasoning for 29 

LLMs [ 39 , 42 ]. The document splitting strategy is a critical factor that determines both the granularity 30 

of evidence selection and the effectiveness of the retrieval model. 31 

The straightforward document splitting method is to segment a full document into a sequence of 32 

flattened pieces [ 21 , 23 ]. However, it fails to capture the inherent structural relationships and semantic 33 

dependencies that exist within the long document, leading to suboptimal retrieval performance [ 26 , 7]. 34 

To this end, several researchers have attempted various strategies to decompose long documents 35 

hierarchically, as illustrated in Figure 1. The most representative work is RAPTOR [ 31 ], which 36

In this work, we present the first approach that 61 

utilizes discourse information for retrieval-based 62 

long document understanding. Specifically, we propose DISRetrieval, which leverages rhetorical 63 

structure theory (RST) to organize long documents for retrieval. RST is a well-established discourse 64 

representation paradigm, using a hierarchical constituent tree to indicate the discourse structure of a 65 

document. The original RST exploits discourse-specific units as the bottom textual nodes. To better 66 

adapt our long document understanding, we ignore the inside-sentence discourse relationships, using 67 

only inter-sentence discourses. Thus, first we convert the original RST trees into sentence-level trees, 68 

and train an off-the-shelf RST model [ 38 ]. Second, we augment the non-leaf RST relation nodes 69 

with textual descriptions using an LLM agent. Finally, we construct an embedding tree based on the 70 

RST structure and employ it for query-based evidence retrieval to support LLM reasoning in long 71 

document understanding. 72 

We conduct experiments on two benchmark datasets, QASPER [ 10 ] (a research paper QA dataset) 73 

and QuALITY [ 27 ] (a long-form reading comprehension dataset), to evaluate the effectiveness of 74 

our method. Results demonstrate that our approach significantly outperforms existing methods 75 

across various metrics. Specifically, our model achieves substantial improvements in both token-76 

level retrieval metrics and downstream question answering tasks. Ablation studies validate the 77 

effectiveness of our discourse-aware tree construction and hierarchical retrieval mechanism, showing 78 

consistent gains over traditional chunking-based methods and flat retrieval approaches. These results 79 

demonstrate that our discourse-aware framework successfully captures both fine-grained semantic 80 

details and document-level patterns, while providing an effective solution for information propagation 81 

across different granularity levels in the hierarchy. 82 

Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: 83 

• We propose a novel discourse-aware hierarchical framework that leverages sentence-level 84 

discourse analysis to create semantically coherent document representations without relying 85 

on arbitrary chunking strategies. 86 

• We develop an innovative bottom-up node representation technique that effectively combines 87 

discourse structure information with LLM-based semantic enhancement. 88 

• Extensive experiments on two challenging datasets demonstrate significant improvements 89 

over existing methods, with up to 4% gains in retrieval metrics and consistent performance 90 

advantages across different model architectures. 91 

22 Related Works 92 

Document Comprehending. Recent advances in natural language processing have revolutionized 93 

document processing and understanding [ 35 , 40 , 3]. While LLMs have demonstrated impressive 94 

capabilities, they face limitations due to context length constraints and memory bottlenecks [ 5 , 8,95 

11 , 1]. Traditional approaches often struggle with long-form content, as they typically operate on 96 

short text segments without considering the broader document context [ 23 , 14 , 30 ]. Dense retrieval 97 

methods, particularly DPR [21], have shown promising results on passage-level tasks but encounter 98 

challenges when scaling to long documents where understanding the overall structure becomes 99 

crucial [ 22 , 15 ]. Several innovative approaches have emerged to address these limitations - SEER 100 

focuses on extracting key information without conventional chunking [ 43 ], while CFIC explores 101 

chunking-free in-context retrieval [ 29 ]. These developments highlight the ongoing evolution in 102 

document comprehension strategies, particularly for handling extended textual content [36, 20, 39]. 103 

Retrieval-based Methods. The retrieval-based paradigm has emerged as a promising solution 104 

for long document understanding, fundamentally operating by first segmenting documents into 105 

manageable pieces before retrieving the most relevant evidence for downstream tasks [ 22 , 21 ,106 

15 , 19 ]. While basic approaches rely on flat sequential splitting, this method fails to capture the 107 

inherent structural relationships and semantic dependencies within documents [ 16 , 3, 40 ]. More 108 

sophisticated tree-based organization methods have been developed, with RAPTOR leading the way 109 

through its implementation of bottom-up semantic clustering [ 31 ]. Alternative approaches include 110 

bisection-based methods that maintain local coherence by grouping adjacent segments [ 41 , 18 ]. 111 

Recent work has also explored hybrid hierarchical retrieval (HHR) and dense hierarchical retrieval 112 

(DHR), establishing connections between document-level and passage-level representations for more 113 

contextually informed retrieval [ 2 , 24 ]. However, these methods generally overlook the linguistic-114 

informed discourse structures naturally present in documents [ 37 , 25 ], leaving room for improvement 115 

in capturing document organization. 116 

3 Methods 117 

Retrieval-based methods have emerged as a promising solution for long document understanding, 118 

where the core idea is to first split documents into smaller pieces and then retrieve the most relevant 119 

evidence for downstream tasks. The most straightforward approach is to segment documents into flat-120 

ten sequences. However, this approach cannot effectively capture the inherent structural relationships 121 

and semantic dependencies within long documents, resulting in suboptimal retrieval performance. 122 

Tree-based document organization methods provide enhanced document representation capabilities. 123 

Representative works like RAPTOR construct document hierarchies through bottom-up semantic 124 

clustering, while bisection-based approaches group adjacent segments to preserve local coherence. 125 

While these methods show improvements, they generally overlook the linguistic-informed discourse 126 

structures that naturally exist in documents. 127 

We propose DISRetrieval, a discourse-aware retrieval framework that explores incorporating discourse 128 

structure into document retrieval. As a case study, we investigate the use of RST, a well-established 129 

discourse representation paradigm, to demonstrate how linguistic structures enhance document 130 

organization and retrieval effectiveness. By leveraging discourse relationships that reflect how 131 

humans organize and comprehend information, our framework maintains semantic coherence and 132 

structural integrity throughout the retrieval process, as illustrated in Figure 2. The algorithm of our 133 

approach is detailed in Algorithm 2 in Appendix C. 134

RST parsing is the task of automatically constructing RST trees from input text. Modern RST parsers 146 

typically employ neural networks to identify discourse boundaries and classify relations between text 147 

spans. These parsers first segment the text into elementary discourse units (EDUs), then incrementally 148 

build the tree structure by determining the relations between adjacent spans and combining them into 149 

larger discourse units. EDUs are the minimal building blocks in RST analysis, typically representing 150 

sub-sentence segments such as clauses. These fine-grained units serve as the leaf nodes of RST trees 151 

and form the basis for identifying discourse relationships in text. 152 

3.1.2 Sentence-level Adaptation 153 

While EDU-level discourse analysis provides fine-grained discourse structure, operating at the 154 

sentence level offers several advantages for long document understanding. First, sentences represent 155 

complete semantic units that maintain better coherence and interpretability compared to sub-sentence 156 

EDUs. Second, sentence-level segmentation significantly reduces computational complexity while 157 

preserving essential discourse relationships. Third, sentences align better with downstream retrieval 158 

tasks where the goal is to identify relevant text segments that can stand independently as evidence. 159 

To enable sentence-level discourse analysis, we train a specialized discourse parser through the 160 

following process: (1) We merge EDUs belonging to the same sentence into single units from existing 161 

EDU-based discourse datasets. (2) For adjacent sentences, we determine their discourse relationship 162 

by identifying the lowest common parent node in the original EDU-based discourse tree. (3) We 163 

preserve these extracted relationships in a new tree structure with complete sentences as basic units. 164 

This conversion process allows us to train a parser fdiscourse that directly operates on sentence-level 165 

relationships while maintaining the essential discourse structure of texts. 166

LLM Enhancement. The document-level discourse tree Tdoc contains only discourse relationships 188 

at internal nodes. To obtain semantic representations for these nodes, we apply bottom-up LLM 189 

enhancement following Equation 2, similar to the paragraph-level process. This enhancement ensures 190 

that all nodes in the tree have meaningful semantic representations. 191 

Tree Integration. After enhancing the document-level tree Tdoc , we perform tree integration to 192 

combine it with all paragraph-level trees Ti into a unified discourse structure. The integration process 193 

is straightforward: each leaf node in Tdoc is replaced with its corresponding paragraph-level discourse 194 

tree Ti, resulting in our final integrated tree TD. This hierarchical structure effectively preserves both 195 

local sentence relationships within paragraphs and global discourse connections across the document. 196 

Node Encoding. To enable efficient retrieval, we transform the discourse tree into an embedding 197 

tree structure using an off-the-shelf pre-trained sentence encoder. The resulting embedding tree 198 

preserves both the hierarchical structure and semantic information of the original discourse tree, 199 

making it suitable for downstream semantic matching tasks. 200 

3.3 Hierarchical Evidence Retrieval and Selection 201 

Our hierarchical evidence retrieval process introduces a novel structure-aware selection mechanism 202 

that leverages both local and global discourse information. Our approach combines: 203 

• Direct selection of highly relevant leaf nodes • Indirect selection through relevant internal nodes • Automatic redundancy elimination via tree structure 

> 204

Specifically, our algorithm first encodes the input query and computes similarity scores with all tree 205 

nodes. Then it processes nodes in order of decreasing relevance: leaf nodes are selected directly 206 

if highly relevant, while for internal nodes, we identify and select the most relevant unselected 207 

leaves from their subtrees. This process continues until we reach the desired evidence set size, 208 

ensuring both local relevance and structural coherence. The complete retrieval process is illustrated 209 

in Figure 2, demonstrating how evidence is gathered from different levels of the discourse tree. For 210 

implementation details, we refer readers to Algorithm 1. 211

4.1 Experimental Setup 213 

Datasets and Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate our approach on two challenging long document 214 

QA datasets: QASPER [ 10 ] for research paper understanding and QuALITY [ 27 ] for long-form 215 

reading comprehension. For QASPER, we use F1-Match for QA performance and token-level 216 

F1/Recall for retrieval evaluation. For QuALITY, we report accuracy as the primary metric. To ensure 217 

fair comparison, we fix the total length of retrieved context across all methods. 218 

Baselines. We evaluate our approach against several strong retrieval baselines: 219 

• Flatten-chunk splits articles into chunks of maximum 100 words while preserving sentence bound-220 

aries for semantic coherence. 221 

• Flatten-sentence adopts sentence-level splitting and direct retrieval, providing a direct comparison 222 

baseline for our hierarchical approach. 223 

• RAPTOR constructs a semantic tree through recursive embedding, clustering, and summarization, 224 

with retrieval performed on a collapsed tree structure following Sarthi et al. [31]. 225 

• Bisection shares our LLM-enhanced representations and hierarchical retrieval mechanism but 226 

uses a different tree construction method - recursively dividing sentence set into balanced binary 227 

subtrees. This baseline helps isolate benefits of our discourse-aware tree structure. 228 

Implementation details. For discourse analysis, we construct our sentence-level RST training 229 

data based on RST-DT [ 6]. We then train our discourse parser following Yu et al. [38] , adopting 230 

XLNet-base-cased as the pre-trained language model backbone. All summarization tasks, including 231 

both our approach (as defined in Equation 2) and RAPTOR baseline, utilize Llama3.1-8B-Instruct 232 

for consistency. We set the temperature parameter τ in Equation 2 to 0 for QASPER and 100 for 233 

QuALITY in our LLM enhancement process. For sentence encoder (Section §3.2), we experiment 234 

with both Sentence-BERT 1 and the latest embedding model of OpenAI 2 to evaluate the effectiveness 235 

of our method across different representation spaces. The Top-K parameter in leaf selection is 236 

set to 5 based on preliminary experiments (detailed in Appendix E.2). For generation models, we 237 

employ UnifiedQA-3B, GPT-4.1-mini and Deepseek-v3 to demonstrate the robustness of our retrieval 238 

improvements across different generation capabilities. More details are provided in Appendix D. 239

Generation Performance. We conduct extensive experiments across different context lengths 241 

(200-400 words) and embedding models (SBERT and OpenAI). The results are shown in Table 1, 242 

comparing our approach with baselines on QASPER and QuALITY datasets. 243 

Our experimental results demonstrate three key findings: 244 

First, DISRetrieval consistently outperforms all baseline methods across different experimental 245 

settings. With 400-word contexts utilizing UnifiedQA-3B, it achieves substantial improvements 246 

over the flatten-sentence baseline (40.25% vs. 37.99% F1-Match on QASPER; 58.91% vs. 55.27% 247

generation models (UnifiedQA-3B, GPT-4.1-mini, and Deepseek-v3). 249

effective than purely relying on semantic-based clustering for document organization. 253 

Finally, ablation studies with the Bisection variant highlight the importance of discourse structure in 254 

our approach. While Bisection improves over flatten-based methods through hierarchical modeling, it 255 

consistently underperforms the full DISRetrieval implementation across all settings. This empirically 256 

confirms that the integration of linguistic-informed discourse structure provides substantial benefits 257 

beyond simple hierarchical organization for long document understanding. 258

12 A Limitation and Future Work 459 

We here discuss the limitations and future work of DISRetrieval. 460 

First, while our discourse-aware tree construction method’s performance is inherently bounded by 461 

the quality of RST parsing, our current approach demonstrates significant improvements even with a 462 

straightforward adaptation from EDU-level to sentence-level RST structures. This success validates 463 

the effectiveness of incorporating discourse information in document retrieval. However, we believe 464 

there is still substantial room for enhancement. In future work, we plan to explore more sophisticated 465 

discourse analysis techniques and develop specialized tree construction methods that can better 466 

capture the hierarchical relationships in long documents. 467 

Second, although we have considered computational efficiency by implementing an adaptive sum-468 

marization strategy with a length threshold τ to determine when LLM processing is necessary, this 469 

approach is relatively simple. While effective, it may not always achieve an optimal balance between 470 

computational cost and representation quality. Future work will investigate more sophisticated 471 

methods for managing computational resources, such as dynamic thresholding based on content com-472 

plexity, hierarchical position-aware summarization, and more efficient LLM architectures specifically 473 

designed for tree-structured document processing. 474 

Third, while our evaluation on QASPER and QuALITY datasets demonstrates significant improve-475 

ments in traditional retrieval metrics, the assessment of long document understanding could benefit 476 

from more diverse evaluation criteria. Current metrics may not fully capture the nuanced aspects of 477 

discourse-aware retrieval, such as structural coherence preservation and hierarchical information flow. 478 

We plan to develop more comprehensive evaluation frameworks that consider multiple dimensions of 479 

retrieval quality, including discourse coherence, semantic relevance, and structural preservation at 480 

different granularity levels. 481 

These limitations point to promising directions for future research while reinforcing the solid foun-482 

dation and significant contributions of our current work. We believe addressing these aspects will 483 

further advance the field of discourse-aware document retrieval and enhance its practical applications. 484

C.1 Discourse Parser Details 514 

The discourse parser architecture builds upon a transition-based system that constructs discourse 515 

trees through a series of well-defined actions. This system is particularly effective for capturing both 516 

local and global discourse relationships while maintaining computational efficiency. 517 

The transition system maintains two primary data structures: a stack σ that holds partially built trees, 518 

and a queue β that contains unprocessed sentences. This design follows the intuition that discourse 519 

relationships often exist between adjacent text spans and can be built incrementally from bottom to 520 

top. 521 

The system builds the tree step by step using three basic operations: 522 

1. A “shift” action moves a sentence from the queue to the stack when we need new content to 523 

process. 524 

2. A “reduce” action combines two adjacent subtrees on top of the stack into a new subtree by 525 

identifying their discourse relationship. 526 

3. A “pop root” action concludes the process when we have successfully built a complete tree. 527 

Each state of the system is represented as c = ( σ, β ), starting from c0 = ([ ] , S i) with all sentences in 528 

the queue, and ending at cf = ([ Ti], [ ]) with a complete discourse tree Ti.529 

The transition system follows a deterministic process guided by the neural scoring model: 530 

1. Initialize σ = [ ] and β = Si.531 

2. While β is not empty or |σ| > 1: (a) If |σ| < 2 and β is not empty, perform a “shift” action 532 

to move the next sentence from β to σ; (b) Else if β is empty, perform a “reduce” action to 533 

combine the top two subtrees in σ; (c) Else, use the neural scoring model to decide between 534 

a “shift” or “reduce” action based on the current state of σ and β.535 

3. Return the single tree Ti remaining on the stack σ.536

1. s1 and s2 are the immediate candidates for the next potential "reduce" action. 539 

2. s3 provides crucial context about the recently built structure. 540

advancement in semantic representation capabilities. 614 

D.3 Implementation Details 615 

In the discourse analysis process, we preserve sentence boundaries throughout all splitting operations 616 

to maintain semantic coherence. For the RAPTOR baseline implementation, we follow the original 617

tree construction process in our Bisection baseline ensures a nearly balanced binary structure through 619 

recursive division of the sentence set. 620
