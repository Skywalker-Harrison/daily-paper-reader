Abstract 

Spoken language models (SLMs) extend LLMs to speech input and output. Existing SLMs rep-resent speech at fixed frame rates (e.g., 25 or 

12 .5 Hz), ignoring the time-varying informa-tion density of speech and offering no flexi-bility to trade off quality for speed at infer-ence time. Recent audio tokenizer research has proposed dynamic frame rate speech cod-ing, which exploits this non-uniformity and enables two new capabilities: very low aver-age frame rates and frame rate controllability. However, this technique has not yet been ap-plied to SLMs. We introduce Flexi ble Spoken 

Language Model (FlexiSLM), the first SLM that supports dynamic and controllable frame rates on both speech input and output. Using dynamic frame rate representations, FlexiSLM outperforms fixed-frame-rate 7B models includ-ing Qwen2.5-Omni and Kimi-Audio at its high-quality operating points. We further verify that FlexiSLM can be accurately steered down to 

4.0 Hz; at 6.25 Hz, it roughly halves inference time relative to 12 .5 Hz while retaining strong speech-to-speech quality. Audio samples are available at https://flexislm.github.io .

FlexiSLM is a thinker-talker speech-in, speech-out model: for output, we reuse FlexiCodec as the talker prediction target; for input, we ap-ply a similar frame-merging strategy to continuous speech representations. For controllable generation, we introduce a conditioning signal that lets the user directly specify the average output frame rate, al-lowing one FlexiSLM to operate at any frame rate

We introduce FlexiSLM, the first dy-namic frame rate SLM framework, with dynamic frame compression on both speech input and out-put. Experiments show strong performance at 

12 .5 Hz and 6.25 Hz, with graceful degradation at 5.0 Hz and 4.0 Hz. 2 Related Work 

Speech Tokenization. Speech tokenization con-verts continuous audio into discrete tokens suit-able for speech language modeling. Early neu-ral audio codecs such as SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2021) and EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2022) use residual vector quantization (RVQ) to produce acoustic tokens at fixed frame rates (e.g., 50 Hz or 75 Hz), prioritizing reconstruction fidelity. Seman-tic tokens derived from self-supervised models like HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) capture linguistic con-     

> 2Code will be released at https://github.com/ AmphionTeam/FlexiSLM .

tent, and are increasingly used in speech language modeling (Borsos et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024a; Ding et al., 2025). Recent work has pushed toward more efficient representations while maintaining high audio quality: single-codebook approaches (50–75 Hz) (Wu et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2024) and semantic-enhanced codecs (12.5–50 Hz) (Li et al., 2025a; Zhang et al., 2023). Recent work has explored dynamic frame rates, leveraging the temporal sparsity of speech so that lower average frame rates reduce the computational cost of speech language models. FlexiCodec (Li et al., 2025b), the tokenizer used in this work, merges 12.5 Hz semantic features based on sim-ilarity to achieve dynamic-rate tokenization at an average of 6.25 Hz. The authors of FlexiCodec also demonstrate controllable frame rate tokenization and TTS. Other dynamic frame rate works, includ-ing CodecSlime (Wang et al., 2025), TFC (Zhang et al., 2025b), and VARSTok (Zheng et al., 2025), explore higher average frame rates from 18.75 Hz to 40 Hz. However, dynamic-rate codecs have not been applied within a spoken language model framework, where low frame rates and frame rate controllability offer larger practical benefits. 

Spoken Language Models. Spoken language models (SLMs) are general-purpose speech pro-cessing systems (Arora et al., 2025). Analogous to text LLMs, they can follow natural-language instructions across diverse speech tasks. Recent end-to-end SLMs extend text-based LLMs to di-rectly comprehend and generate speech. A domi-nant design follows a three-stage paradigm: a pre-trained speech encoder extracts acoustic features, which condition a decoder-only LLM, followed by an additional transformer module or prediction head that predicts speech tokens (Wang et al., 2026; Xu et al., 2025a; Ding et al., 2025). Kimi-Audio (Ding et al., 2025) models parallel speech-text at 12.5 Hz using a separate LM head for speech tokens. Qwen2.5-Omni (Xu et al., 2025a) adopts a thinker– talker architecture operating at 25 Hz, whose talker module predicts speech tokens. Active research areas include full-duplex capability (e.g., Interac-tion Models (Défossez et al., 2024)), interleaved speech-text sequences (e.g., GLM-4-Voice (Zeng et al., 2024)), low-frame-rate audio tokenization for SLMs (e.g., Moshi (Défossez et al., 2024)), and dual-resolution speech representations (e.g., Fun-Audio-Chat/DrVoice (Tan et al., 2025b,a) and Mimo-Audio (Zhang et al., 2025a) group speech Audio Decoder

3 Method 

3.1 Architecture Overview 

Figure 2 illustrates the overall architecture of Flex-iSLM, a parallel speech-text model with the follow-ing components: 

Audio Encoder. This module encodes the user’s speech into a semantic-rich continuous representa-tion suitable for LLM understanding. We adopt the pretrained Qwen2.5-Omni audio encoder (Xu et al., 2025a), which extracts 25 Hz continuous speech features from waveforms. 

Frame Merging Module. This module com-presses the number of frames in a sequence. It appears twice in our model: (1) on the input side, it reduces the 25 Hz continuous features from the Au-dio Encoder to a dynamic-rate sequence ≤12.5 Hz; (2) inside the pretrained FlexiCodec audio tok-enizer, it merges 12.5 Hz ASR features before quantization. Both instances share the same merg-ing mechanism. We describe this module in Sec-tion 3.2. 

LLM Backbone (Thinker). We use Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2024) as initialization. This LLM has also been used in Qwen2.5-Omni and Kimi-Audio to initialize their backbones. 

FlexiCodec Audio Tokenizer. We use the open-source pretrained FlexiCodec to obtain discrete speech tokens as the prediction target of Flex-iSLM’s Talker module. As illustrated in Fig-ure 2(a), the codec discretizes each frame with Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ (Mentzer et al., 2023)); each token is paired with a frame length attribute for audio reconstruction. We use Flexi-Codec’s semantic tokens and omit its RVQ acoustic tokens. Appendix H provides more details. 

Talker Transformer. The Talker decodes the Thinker LLM’s hidden states and outputs into Flex-iCodec’s dynamic-frame-rate speech tokens. • Input: As shown in Figure 3, its input sequence runs over the entire user + assistant context. At each position, the Talker input embedding is pro-jected from the concatenation of (1) the backbone LLM’s last-layer hidden state, (2) a sinusoidal embedding of the target frame rate (Section 3.3), and (3) the embeddings of the previously emitted speech and frame length tokens. • Output: The Talker produces two parallel output streams: FlexiCodec FSQ codes and their associ-ated frame lengths, enabling dynamic-rate output. The Talker uses two output LM heads to predict the streams in parallel. • Token delay: As shown in Figure 3, the Talker’s FSQ token stream is delayed by 5 tokens from the text stream. This provides a small lookahead that prevents speech from preceding its correspond-ing text (Du et al., 2024b). The frame length tokens are delayed by an additional one position, allowing the model to predict a frame’s duration after knowing its corresponding speech token (Li et al., 2025b).

Talker-to-Thinker Connection. In addition to the standard cascaded Thinker-to-Talker informa-tion flow (Xu et al., 2025a), FlexiSLM contains an optional Talker-to-Thinker connection (Tan et al., 2025a) (the red dashed arrow in Figure 2) that feeds the Talker’s previously emitted speech-token em-beddings back into the Thinker LLM Backbone at the next step. This gives the Thinker LLM explicit access to what has already been spoken. The con-nection projects the concatenation of the Talker’s embeddings (speech code + frame length) and the text embedding into the Thinker LLM’s hidden state. We can disable this connection by zeroing out the contribution of the Talker’s embeddings in the concatenation. 

3.2 Frame Merging Module 

As shown in Figure 2(b), the Frame Merging Mod-ule adaptively compresses a fixed-rate semantic feature sequence by merging adjacent frames that carry redundant information. Given a sequence of feature vectors x1, x2, . . . , xT at a base frame rate, we compute the cosine similarity between consec-utive frames: st = xt·xt+1 

∥xt∥ ∥ xt+1 ∥ , t = 1 , . . . , T −1.

If st exceeds a merging threshold τ , frames xt and 

xt+1 are grouped and their average is computed. This process runs greedily from left to right, with contiguous high-similarity frames merged into a single averaged representation. After merging, each group produces an averaged feature ¯xk and a frame length attribute lk denoting the number of original frames in the group. We in-terleave the original and averaged features to form an augmented sequence, which is processed by a lightweight Transformer with local attention. Fi-nally, we retrieve the representations at positions corresponding to the averaged features, yielding the merged sequence with associated frame lengths. 

3.3 Controllable Frame Rate 

A key feature of FlexiSLM is its ability to control the output frame rate at inference time, enabling a single deployed model to operate across a range of compute budgets without retraining. For a dy-namic frame rate sequence, the average frame rate is defined as Total number of frames after merging Audio duration in seconds . We first describe a baseline threshold-based strategy and its limitations, then introduce our proposed direct frame rate control. We focus on output frame rate control; input frame rate is controlled by comput-ing the target number of merged frames from the desired rate and selecting a merging threshold per utterance to match it. 

Merging Threshold Control (Baseline). Astraightforward approach is to control the merg-ing threshold τ : a higher τ merges fewer frames (higher frame rate), while a lower τ merges more (lower frame rate). This approach is used in FlexiCodec-TTS (Li et al., 2025b). However, it provides only indirect control, with several limi-tations: (1) the resulting frame rate varies signifi-cantly across utterances and datasets, making the speedup difficult to predict (Table 4); (2) it is a one-to-many mapping: a single threshold maps to a wide distribution of average frame rates, increasing modeling complexity; and (3) it is unintuitive for users unfamiliar with the model architecture.

3.4 Training Procedure 

Training proceeds in three stages: 

Stage 1: Talker Pre-training. In this stage, we freeze the LLM backbone and train only the ran-domly initialized Talker. We train FlexiSLM end to end with TTS data. This stage uses English sam-ples from Emilia (He et al., 2024) and Multilingual LibriSpeech (MLS) (Pratap et al., 2020), totaling about 100K training hours. The Talker-to-Thinker connection is disabled in Stages 1 and 2. 

Stage 2: Multi-Task LoRA Fine-Tuning. In this stage, we activate the speech input-side Frame Merging Module, Thinker, and Talker. LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) is applied to the Thinker. The Frame Merging Module is randomly initialized. We train on a mixture of speech processing tasks. Ta-ble 2 shows the distribution of the data sources in Stage 2. A central component is FlexiSLM-Data ,a speech-to-speech dialogue corpus constructed for this work. FlexiSLM-Data is distilled from a 30B Qwen3-Omni (Xu et al., 2025b) SLM, with data sourced from a wide range of datasets. We detail our data construction process in Appendix A. 

Stage 3: Full Fine-Tuning. This stage further un-locks the backbone’s capacity and improves speech perception. It continues from the Stage 2 check-point and uses the same data, with two differ-ences. First, we perform full-parameter training after merging the LoRA updates back into the tar-get LLM weight matrices. Second, we enable and train the Talker-to-Thinker connection. 

3.5 Training Loss and Configuration 

Our model is trained end to end with a weighted cross-entropy loss over multiple token streams. The total loss is: L = λtext Ltext + λspeech Lspeech +

λspeech_len Lspeech_len , where Ltext , Lspeech , and 

Lspeech_len are the cross-entropy losses for text tokens, FlexiCodec speech codes, and per-token speech frame lengths, respectively. We set λtext =2 and λspeech = λspeech_len = 1 . For non-speech sequences, Lspeech and Lspeech_len are set to 0. We train FlexiSLM on 24 A100 80G GPUs. Dur-ing training, we randomly sample an input frame rate target from U(4 , 12 .5) Hz and a FlexiCodec merging threshold τ ∼ U (0 .85 , 1.0) to support multiple frame rates. The Talker has 630M param-eters; the Audio Encoder has 640M parameters; each Merging Transformer has 20M parameters. We discuss more training details in Appendix C.

4.3 Frame Rate Controllability 4.3.1 Frame Rate Control Accuracy 

We evaluate how accurately FlexiSLM can control the average output frame rate. We compare two variants that differ only in their control mechanism: merging threshold control and direct frame rate control. Table 4 reports the resulting average output frame rates across benchmark datasets. We focus on the output side because the input frame rate is controlled deterministically and therefore does not require a separate analysis. 

• Direct frame rate control is more accurate than threshold. Merging threshold control ex-hibits high per-utterance variance: for example, targeting roughly 8 Hz via τ =0 .90 yields a wide range of frame rates ( 3.91 ~10 .74 Hz, σ≈0.70 ) and varying averages across datasets ( 7.91 ~8.34 Hz). In contrast, direct frame-rate control, used in our final FlexiSLM, closely matches the requested av-erage rates, with errors below 0.1 Hz across all settings. 

• When might merging threshold control still be preferable? One could argue that a dynamic frame-rate system should adapt to utterance-level redundancy, and that the higher variance of thresh-old control reflects this adaptivity. We view the two strategies as complementary. Threshold con-trol fixes the compression intensity applied to local frame pairs, allowing the resulting frame rate to vary with the redundancy of each utterance. Direct frame-rate control instead fixes the average frame rate of each utterance and adjusts the effective com-pression intensity accordingly. 4.3.2 Frame Rate and Inference Efficiency 

Table 5 reports the real-time factor (RTF) of FlexiSLM and Qwen2.5-Omni-7B under different frame rates. RTF is computed as the ratio of total processing time to generated audio duration; lower is faster. All measurements are conducted on a sin-gle A100 GPU with batch size 1, and averaged over 

30 runs on an OpenAudioBench subset. The same audio prompts are used for all FlexiSLM configura-tions, with an average prompt duration of 7.7 s. We also report an approximate model-side compute es-timate based on the sequence length and parameter count of each module. This estimate excludes the non-autoregressive flow-matching audio decoder for both systems, since both decoders operate on similar 50 Hz acoustic sequences and their paral-lel decoding cost is not strongly reflected in the measured RTF on a high-end GPU. 

• Output frame rate is the main driver of in-ference speedup. Reducing the output frame rate from 12 .5 to 6.25 Hz nearly halves the RTF (1.17 → 0.59 ), since autoregressive speech gen-eration dominates the total inference cost. In contrast, reducing the input frame rate from 12 .5

to 6.25 Hz yields only a modest improvement (1.17 → 1.09 ), indicating that input prefilling ac-counts for a smaller fraction of total inference time. The TFLOPs estimates show the same effect: hold-ing the input rate fixed at 12 .5 Hz, reducing the out-put rate from 12 .5 to 6.25 Hz lowers FlexiSLM’s model-side compute from 4.57 to 3.41 TFLOPs. 

• A single FlexiSLM covers a wide range of operating points. By varying the input and out-put frame rates, the same model covers operat-ing points from 1.17 to 0.57 RTF without retrain-ing. This gives FlexiSLM a practical speed-quality tradeoff control that fixed-rate SLMs do not pro-vide. 

• Comparison with Qwen2.5-Omni-7B. At the 

12 .5/12 .5 Hz setting, FlexiSLM is 1.3× faster than Qwen2.5-Omni-7B ( 1.17 vs. 1.57 RTF). When the FlexiSLM output frame rate is reduced to 

6.25 Hz, the speedup increases to 2.7×. This gain is smaller than the raw output-rate ratio between Qwen2.5-Omni and FlexiSLM ( 50 vs. 6.25 Hz) because the two systems use different inference structures: FlexiSLM’s Thinker processes a speech-length sequence to maintain bidirectional Thinker– Talker information flow, whereas Qwen2.5-Omni’s Thinker stops after text generation. FlexiSLM’s design trades part of the rate-reduction speedup for stronger speech-aware response generation, and is also aligned with future full-duplex and interactive SLM settings.

6 Conclusion 

We presented FlexiSLM, to our knowledge the first spoken language model with dynamic and con-trollable frame rates. FlexiSLM applies dynamic frame rates to both speech input and output, and introduces direct frame rate conditioning so that a single model operates from 12 .5 down to 4.0 Hz without retraining. Experiments show that Flex-iSLM achieves the best speech-to-speech QA score among 7B SLMs at 12 .5 Hz and remains stronger in s2s than fixed-rate 7B baselines at 6.25 Hz. More aggressive 5.0 and 4.0 Hz settings provide addi-tional low-token operating points with larger but smooth quality degradation. Halving the output frame rate halves inference time with only 1.0-point s2s loss. FlexiSLM demonstrates a practical paradigm for efficient spoken language models. 

Limitations 

FlexiSLM has several limitations. First, we have not explored post-training techniques such as RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) or DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), which could further improve response quality and alignment. Second, the current model is not a streaming model; adapting the audio de-coder for causal operation is an important next step for FlexiSLM to support real-time conver-sation. Third, our training data does not cover reasoning-intensive tasks, multi-turn dialogues, or many multiple-choice questions, limiting general-ization to these scenarios.

A Construction of FlexiSLM-Data 

As described in Section 3.4 and Table 2, FlexiSLM is trained in Stages 2 and 3 on a mixture of tasks, with an emphasis on speech-to-speech dialogue data: • Speech-to-speech (s2s) dialogue : mainly 

FlexiSLM-Data (1.4M samples). • TTS : Emilia (He et al., 2024), MLS (Pratap et al., 2020). • ASR : MLS and LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015). • Audio understanding : LLaSO-Instruct (Sun et al., 2025) covering emotion, accent, vocal recognition, etc. • Text-to-text (t2t) dialogue : TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017), WebQuestions (Berant et al., 2013). This section details the pipeline for building a large-scale speech-to-speech dialogue dataset, which we call FlexiSLM-Data . The current dataset is limited to single-turn interactions. The construc-tion pipeline has the following steps: • Prompt collection. We collect text prompts from public QA, instruction-following, and dialogue datasets (Table 7). For multi-turn datasets, we use the user’s first-turn utterance as the prompt, skipping samples whose first turn is a generic greeting (e.g., “Hello”). • Response generation. We use Qwen3-Omni (Xu et al., 2025b) to generate text responses for these prompts. We choose a spoken language model rather than a text-only LLM because Qwen3-Omni produces speech-friendly responses that are short, conversational, and free of formatting that does not transfer to speech (e.g., bullet points, code blocks, or long enumerations). We feed the 

text version of the prompt rather than its syn-thesized speech, since text-input responses are typically more accurate. • Speech synthesis. The generated responses are synthesized into speech by Qwen3-TTS (Hu et al., 2026) with a fixed speaker “Ryan”. The speech prompts are synthesized using Fish-Audio TTS 3 (Liao et al., 2024), with speaker prompts randomly sampled from English Emilia utter-ances longer than 5 seconds (He et al., 2024). This produces 4M samples with a total audio duration of 30K hours. • Quality filtering. We apply format-based fil-tering to remove samples containing code, for-mulas, excessive punctuation, or non-target lan-guages; correctness filtering using the DeepSeek-V4-Flash API; and ASR-based filtering with Whisper-medium to discard cases with WER greater than 20%. • Final scale. After filtering, we obtain 1.4Mspeech-to-speech dialogue samples, totaling 

9.9K hours of speech ( 2.7K hours of user prompts and 7.2K hours of assistant responses). Sample prompt-response pairs from FlexiSLM-Data are shown as text transcriptions in Table 9.

G Flow-Matching Audio Decoder Details 

FlexiSLM uses the audio decoder provided in the open-source FlexiCodec repository 4. The decoder is a two-stage pipeline: a VoiceBox (Le et al., 2023)-style NAR flow matching model followed by a vocoder. The VoiceBox model has 363M pa-rameters, and the vocoder has The NAR flow matching model takes Flexi-Codec’s FSQ semantic tokens and associated frame lengths as input, and generates mel spectrograms conditioned on a short reference audio prompt that defines the target speaker identity. The model input is a 12.5 Hz fixed-frame-rate sequence interpolated to 50 Hz to match the mel-spectrogram rate. To construct the 12.5 Hz sequence, each FlexiCodec FSQ semantic token is repeated according to its frame length. The flow matching model learns a velocity field vθ(zt, t ) that transports samples from a Gaussian prior z0 ∼ N (0 , I ) to the mel-spectrogram distribution z1 under the conditioning inputs. The mel spectrograms are then converted to 24 kHz waveforms by a pretrained Vocos (Siuzdak, 2023) vocoder.

Dynamic Frame Rate via Frame Merging. The Frame Merging Module is applied to the 12.5 Hz ASR features. Adjacent frames whose cosine simi-larity exceeds a threshold τ are merged, producing a variable-frame length sequence. The merged fea-tures are then quantized via FSQ (in the semantic-encoding branch) or RVQ (in the acoustic-encoding branch). By varying τ from 0.8 to 1.0, FlexiCodec supports average frame rates from 3 Hz to 12.5 Hz. At 12.5 Hz no merging occurs and FlexiCodec be-haves as a standard fixed-rate codec. 

I Future Work 

While FlexiSLM validates dynamic and control-lable frame rates inside an SLM, several directions remain open and motivate our future work: 

• Full-duplex and interactive SLMs. The Talker-to-Thinker connection introduced in Section 3.1 routes the actually-rendered speech tokens back to the Thinker LLM, which gives the Thinker a coherent acoustic context across turns. This is a natural foundation for full-duplex SLMs that can listen, think, and speak concurrently. We plan to extend our FlexiSLM framework to full duplex in future research. 

• Streaming and on-device deployment. The cur-rent FlexiSLM is non-streaming on both input and output. Adapting the Frame Merging Module and audio decoder to causal or chunk-based streaming, combined with FlexiSLM’s frame rate controllabil-ity, would enable real-time conversation and let a single model match heterogeneous compute bud-gets from cloud to edge devices. 

• Post-training for spoken dialogue. We have not yet explored RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022) or DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) on top of FlexiSLM. Spoken-dialogue-specific reward models, including prosody, naturalness, and turn-taking signals, are likely to improve response quality beyond what supervised distillation alone can provide. 

• Reasoning, multi-turn, and tool-use coverage. 

FlexiSLM-Data is dominated by single-turn factual and instruction-following responses. Extending the data construction pipeline to multi-turn dialogues, chain-of-thought reasoning, and tool-augmented interactions would broaden the model’s capabil-ity surface while keeping the same dynamic-rate backbone. 

• Unified low-rate audio modeling. The frame-merging and frame rate-conditioning machinery is not specific to speech. Applying the same control-lable dynamic-rate recipe to general audio (music, environmental sound, mixed audio) is a promising direction toward unified, efficient audio language models.
