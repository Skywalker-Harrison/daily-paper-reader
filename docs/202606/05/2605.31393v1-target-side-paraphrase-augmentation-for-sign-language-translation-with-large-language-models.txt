## Abstract 

Sign language translation (SLT) remains constrained by lim-ited paired sign-video/text corpora and heavy-tailed target vocabularies. We study target-side augmentation in which GPT-4o generates controlled paraphrase variants of refer-ence sentences while the sign input remains unchanged. A Signformer-style pose-based Transformer is trained under a two-stage schedule: pre-training on the augmented corpus followed by fine-tuning on the original references. We evaluate on three datasets spanning complementary challenges: PHOENIX14T (German Sign Language), with moderate lexical diversity; GSL (Greek Sign Language), with highly controlled, repetitive recordings; and LSA-T (Argen-tinian Sign Language), with severe long-tail sparsity. On PHOENIX14T, augmentation improves BLEU-4 from 9.56 to 10.33. The near-saturated GSL baseline and extremely sparse LSA-T setting reveal the limits of the approach. To our knowledge, this is the first study to apply LLM-generated target-side paraphrases and LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation to SLT. The semantic evaluation reveals gains in fidelity that lexical overlap metrics understate. 

## 1. Introduction 

Sign language translation (SLT) maps sign-language video to spoken-language text, sitting at the intersection of computer vision and NLP. Despite substantial recent progress [ 4, 5], robust translation remains difficult due to the multimodal complexity of signed utterances, the scarcity of large parallel corpora [ 3], and heavy-tailed target vocabularies where many words appear only a handful of times. These constraints motivate compact, pose-based mod-els. We adopt a Signformer-inspired architecture [ 17 ] us-ing MediaPipe-derived pose features, so that the effect of data augmentation can be studied within a controlled and resource-efficient setup. Data augmentation is standard in low-resource MT [ 8, 13 ], but in SLT it has mostly targeted the sign side [ 11 , 15 ]. We instead augment the text side : each reference sentence is rewritten into semantically faithful variants via an LLM, exposing the decoder to multiple acceptable surface realizations of the same signed content. To our knowledge, LLM-generated target-side paraphrase augmentation has not previously been studied for SLT, although LLMs have ap-peared in other SLT roles [16]. We evaluate on PHOENIX14T [ 4], GSL [ 1], and LSA-T [ 2]. We also conduct a semantic evaluation using an LLM-as-a-Judge protocol (Section 5).

Our contributions: (1) we introduce LLM-based target-side paraphrase augmentation for SLT and release three augmented datasets covering DGS, GSL, and LSA; (2) we present a three-dataset study showing that the effect depends strongly on corpus characteristics; and (3) we conduct a se-mantic evaluation using LLM-as-a-Judge on PHOENIX14T and GSL, revealing gains that lexical overlap metrics under-state. All code and datasets are publicly available 1.

## 2. Related Work 

Sign Language Translation. Early SLT systems used a two-stage pipeline: sign language recognition predicted glosses, which were then translated to text [ 4]. More re-cent work has shifted toward gloss-free SLT, mapping video directly to text [ 5, 6]. Modern systems rely on transformer ar-chitectures; Sign2GPT [ 16 ] combines CLIP with a GPT-style decoder, while Signformer [ 17 ] achieves competitive perfor-mance with a lightweight transformer. Our model follows this efficiency-oriented line using body-pose sequences. 

Data Augmentation in SLT. Prior SLT augmentation has focused on the sign side through production models [ 14 ]or generative methods [ 15 ]. Target-side augmentation has received less attention: [ 11 ] used heuristic paraphrase rules for gloss-to-text data. In NLP more broadly, LLMs have been used for paraphrase generation in low-resource tasks [ 7]. Our work brings that idea to SLT, keeping sign input fixed while rewriting target references. 

## 3. Methodology 

3.1. Model Architecture 

Our baseline follows the Signformer encoder–decoder Trans-former [ 17 ] (Figure 1), adapted to accept skeleton keypoints. MediaPipe Holistic [ 10 ] extracts 2D coordinates for 33 body landmarks, 21 per hand, and a facial subset, concatenated into a per-frame feature vector projected into the model’s embedding space through a linear layer. This pose-based representation emphasizes articulatory motion over appearance, suppressing background and light-ing variation. While pose-based approaches may lag behind image-based models [ 19 ], the reduced dimensionality sim-plifies the pipeline and the relative comparisons with and without augmentation remain informative within this setting.

3.3. Training Schedule 

We compare two conditions: • Baseline: trained on the original, non-augmented training set. • +Augmentation: – Stage 1: pre-train on the augmented corpus (originals plus three paraphrases per instance). 

– Stage 2: fine-tune on originals only, re-aligning the decoder with reference phrasing. All hyperparameters and early stopping remain identical across conditions. The two-stage schedule is central: pre-training broadens the decoder distribution, while fine-tuning re-centers it on the reference style without discarding the broader lexical expo-sure. Both conditions use teacher forcing with cross-entropy loss, warm-up-and-decay learning rate, label smoothing, and early stopping on validation loss.

GSL starts from 94.38 BLEU; augmentation slightly reduces it to 92.22, as the decoder produces semantically valid but lexically different outputs that single-reference BLEU penalizes. LSA-T remains at 1.2 BLEU in both con-ditions, as target-side augmentation cannot address sign-side data sparsity.

5.2. Semantic Evaluation via LLM-as-a-Judge 

To address this mismatch, we evaluate PHOENIX14T and GSL with an LLM judge scoring semantic fidelity and lin-guistic quality. Prior work supports this direction: LLM judges show high agreement with human preferences [ 20 ], and LLM-based translation evaluators achieve competitive correlation with human judgments [9]. 

Results. Table 4 reports semantic fidelity. On PHOENIX14T, augmentation raises fidelity from 2.51 to 3.65 (+45%), fluent-but-wrong translations drop from 54.8% to 35.5%, and pairwise preferences favor augmentation 52.9% vs. 13.1%. On GSL, fidelity improves from 7.72 to 8.77 (+13.6%), confirming semantic gains where lexical metrics are near-saturated. 

Limitations. GPT-5.2 served as judge while GPT-4o gen-erated augmented targets. Although architecturally distinct, shared training lineage may introduce subtle alignment; se-mantic judging should be treated as complementary evidence, ideally validated with human assessment. 

## 6. Conclusion 

Target-side LLM augmentation is not uniformly benefi-cial for SLT: its value depends on where a corpus sits along the spectrum from formulaic to long-tail sparse. On PHOENIX14T, paraphrastic exposure improves both BLEU and semantic fidelity. On GSL, the near-perfect baseline leaves no room for lexical variation to help. On LSA-T, target-side rewriting cannot address sign-side sparsity. The semantic evaluation confirms gains exceeding what BLEU captures. Promising directions include combining target-text with sign-side augmentation, evaluating with mul-tiple references, and replacing GPT-4o with open-source alternatives to clarify whether gains stem from the augmen-tation principle or the specific generator.
