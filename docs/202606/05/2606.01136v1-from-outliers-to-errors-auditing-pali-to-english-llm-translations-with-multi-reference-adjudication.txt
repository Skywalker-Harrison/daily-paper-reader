# From Outliers to Errors: Auditing Pali-to-English LLM Translations with Multi-Reference Adjudication 

Máté Metzger, Independent Researcher, Hungary Nadnapang Phophichit, Nibbana Meditation Centre, Thailand Hansa Dhammahaso, Nibbana Meditation Centre, Thailand 

Abstract 

Single-score translation metrics can conflate legitimate variation with error, a problem especially acute for classical languages where multiple defensible English renderings of the same passage co-exist. We audit Pali-to-English output from four flagship large language models (LLMs)–GPT-5.5, Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and Grok 4.3–on 1,700 passages from the Pali Canon, using three established human translations by Bhikkhu Sujato, Thanissaro Bhikkhu, and Bhikkhu Bodhi as a lo-cal reference envelope rather than a single gold standard. Each candidate’s normalized embedding drift from the reference centroid serves as a triage signal, not an error label; the 1,203 candidates above a 1.5 drift threshold are then adjudicated by a blinded three-model LLM judge panel, cali-brated against a 300-instance author-adjudicated validation set. Two results stand out. First, drift predicts severity rather than error per se: the major-error rate among adjudicated high-drift candi-dates rose monotonically from 7.9% in the 1.5-2.0 band to 51.6% above 3.0 , while approximately 80% of 1.5-2.0 outliers were judged valid translation variations. Second, model differences were clearest in the high-drift tail: GPT-5.5 had the lowest adjudicated high-drift major-error rate, with confidence intervals overlapping those of Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 3.1 Pro; Grok 4.3 had both the largest outlier volume and the highest tail major-error rate (27.6% overall, 74.4% above drift 3. 0). The dominant major-error categories–omission or truncation, doctrinal term errors, agent or role errors, additions or hallucinations, and negation or polarity errors–are precisely the failures most likely to mislead readers of doctrinal text. The contribution is a reusable audit design for classical-to-modern translation: define a local reference envelope from multiple human translators, use em-bedding drift to prioritize review, and adjudicate the flagged tail with calibrated severity labels rather than treating outlier status as error. 

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly plausible translators for texts and language pairs that sit outside the center of standard machine-translation benchmarks. Recent studies find that Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT)-class systems can perform competitively on some translation directions, while still showing strong variation by language, domain, prompt, and resource level [Jiao et al. 2023]; [Hendy et al. 2023]; [Zhu et al. 2024]; [Xu et al. 2024]. At the same time LLMs are evolving rapidly. This makes translation of classical and low-resource languages a particularly important test case. These languages often have limited parallel data, specialized terminology, historical registers, and interpretive traditions that are poorly represented by modern web text. Pali is a Middle Indo-Aryan language best known as the language of the Pali Canon, the scriptural corpus of Theravada Buddhism. Pali canonical texts are formulaic in some places, dense and elliptical in others, and often depend on a technical Buddhist vocabulary whose English rendering is both consequential and legitimately variable. Human translators differ in style, compression, terminology, and interpretive 1

stance. A translation can diverge from one reference and still be defensible; conversely, a fluent and reference-like translation can contain a substantive error. Part of this problem can be addressed by using multiple independent translations as references. In this study, the selected references are not interchangeable targets but works by translators with distinct styles, audiences, and interpretive commitments: Bhikkhu Sujato’s plain and accessible digital reading environ-ment on SuttaCentral, Thanissaro Bhikkhu’s practice-oriented corpus, and Bhikkhu Bodhi’s scholarly editions. Since translation is an interpretive and culturally situated act rather than a transparent trans-fer of meaning [Venuti 2018]; [Tymoczko 2014], the three references are treated here as interpretive witnesses that help define a range of defensible renderings, not as a neutral oracle. Multi-reference evaluation is especially relevant for texts where translation variation is not noise but evidence of the range of acceptable renderings [Fomicheva, Specia, and Guzmán 2020]; [Wu, Wieting, and Smith 2024]. However, multi-reference metrics still do not answer a central scholarly question: when an LLM translation falls outside the human-reference envelope, is it wrong, or merely different? This paper treats that question as the object of study. The main contribution is a two-stage audit framework. First, candidate translations are embedded and compared against the local distribution of human references. This produces a normalized drift score that functions as a triage signal. Second, high-drift candidates are adjudicated with explicit labels for valid variation, minor error, and major error. The distinction matters. Automatic metrics such as Bilingual Evaluation Understudy (BLEU), character n-gram F-score (chrF), learned neural metrics, and embedding similarity are useful summaries, but prior work has repeatedly warned against treating scalar scores as direct substitutes for human error analysis [Papineni et al. 2002]; [Popović 2015]; [Rei et al. 2020]; [Freitag et al. 2022]; [Mathur et al. 2020]. Our design therefore asks not only which model is closer to references on average, but which outputs contain correction-worthy or materially misleading translation errors, and which are valid renderings despite differing from the references. The study evaluates four flagship translator models on 1,700 selected Pali passages and adjudicates the high-drift portion of their outputs with a calibrated LLM judge panel. The goal is not to claim that LLM judges replace expert philological assessment. Instead, the goal is to test whether a scalable, reference-aware audit pipeline can separate legitimate variation from likely errors well enough to estimate error rates, error types, and model-specific failure patterns. The resulting blueprint is intended to generalize to other classical-to-modern language settings where multiple human references exist but exhaustive expert review is impractical. The paper is organized around four research questions. First, how strongly does multi-reference em-bedding drift predict minor and major translation errors? Second, among high-drift translations, what proportion are genuine errors rather than valid variations? Third, what types of Pali-to-English errors do flagship LLMs make, and do those types differ across models? Fourth, do serious failures cluster on the same difficult source passages, or distributed more broadly? These questions shift the evaluation target from model ranking alone to error discovery, severity estimation, and reusable audit design. 

# 2 Literature Review 

Machine-translation evaluation has long moved between automatic scoring and human error analysis. BLEU established the dominant early paradigm of corpus-level n-gram overlap [Papineni et al. 2002], while later lexical and character-level metrics such as Metric for Evaluation of Translation with Explicit ORdering (METEOR) and chrF addressed some of BLEU’s brittleness, especially around morphology and partial matching [Lavie and Denkowski 2009]; [Popović 2015]; [Popović 2017]. Subsequent work pushed evaluation toward contextual and learned semantic metrics, including BERTScore, Bilingual Evaluation Understudy with Representations from Transformers (BLEURT), and Crosslingual Optimized 2Metric for Evaluation of Translation (COMET) [Zhang et al. 2020]; [Sellam, Das, and Parikh 2020]; [Rei et al. 2020]. The Workshop on Machine Translation (WMT) metrics literature suggests that neural met-rics often correlate better with human judgments than BLEU, but also stresses that metric choice and reporting details can change conclusions [Post 2018]; [Freitag et al. 2022]; [Mathur et al. 2020]. This paper builds on that literature but uses automatic metrics differently. Rather than using a scalar metric as the final outcome, we use multi-reference embedding distance as an audit filter. This choice follows two observations. First, multiple references better represent acceptable translation variation than a single reference, particularly for literary or interpretive texts [Fomicheva, Specia, and Guzmán 2020]; [Wu, Wieting, and Smith 2024]. Second, embedding models can capture semantic proximity beyond surface overlap, but they are not neutral arbiters of translation correctness. Sentence and multilingual embedding work has made semantic comparison more practical [Reimers and Gurevych 2019]; [Artetxe and Schwenk 2019]; [Feng et al. 2022], while embedding benchmark studies caution that no embedding method dominates every task [Muennighoff et al. 2023]. In this study, embedding drift is therefore a way to find suspicious cases, not a way to declare them erroneous. The adjudication layer draws on translation quality assessment and error typology traditions. Multidimen-sional Quality Metrics (MQM)-style evaluation emphasizes explicit error categories, severity, and expert judgment rather than undifferentiated adequacy scores [Lommel 2018]; [MQM Council n.d.]. Large-scale human machine-translation (MT) evaluation has shown that expert error annotation with context can expose quality differences that simpler ratings or automatic metrics obscure [Freitag et al. 2021]. Error-focused automatic metrics such as xCOMET similarly reflect a broader movement from global scores toward transparent error detection [Guerreiro et al. 2024]. Our labels are intentionally coarser than full MQM: they separate valid variation, minor error, and major error, then assign a limited set of categories designed for Pali-to-English translation. This narrower taxonomy is meant to support reliable adjudication at scale while preserving the distinction most important for downstream users: whether a reader would be materially misled. Related literature also discusses hallucination and severe translation failures. Early MT error analysis already argued that aggregate metrics do not identify the main sources of system failure [Vilar et al. 2006]. Neural MT and LLM translation add renewed concern about hallucinated, omitted, or semantically unrelated output [Raunak, Menezes, and Junczys-Dowmunt 2021]; [Guerreiro et al. 2023a]; [Guerreiro et al. 2023b]; [Dale et al. 2023]; [Benkirane et al. 2024]. These studies are directly relevant to our distinction between minor and major errors. A minor terminology preference may be correction-worthy but not misleading; however, an omission, polarity reversal, wrong agent, or hallucinated addition can change the reader’s understanding of the passage. The present study asks whether such serious failures cluster in the high-drift tail and whether their categories differ by model. LLM-based judging is the other methodological pillar. GPT Estimation Metric Based Assessment (GEMBA) and GEMBA-MQM showed that LLMs can be prompted to score MT quality and identify MQM-like error spans [Kocmi and Federmann 2023a]; [Kocmi and Federmann 2023b]. Work such as G-Eval and MT-Bench/Chatbot Arena further demonstrates the utility and risks of LLM-as-judge designs for language-generation evaluation [Liu et al. 2023]; [Zheng et al. 2023]; [Chiang et al. 2024]. The risk is particularly acute when the judge is asked to adjudicate subtle translation variation in a specialized domain. For that reason, our LLM panel is independent of the translator panel, judges are blinded to translator identity, and a 300-instance author-adjudicated validation set is used to measure calibration before interpreting the full result set. Finally, the study is situated within computational work on ancient and low-resource languages. No Lan-guage Left Behind (NLLB) and FLORES Evaluation Benchmark for Low-Resource and Multilingual Translation (FLORES) demonstrate the scale and importance of multilingual translation benchmarks, but classical languages remain underrepresented in mainstream MT evaluation [Costa-jussà et al. 2022]; [Costa-jussà et al. 2024]; [Goyal et al. 2022]. Ancient-language natural language processing (NLP) has developed specialized models and tasks for Greek, Latin, Sanskrit, and Buddhist textual traditions, in-3cluding Sanskrit morphological disambiguation and unified Sanskrit NLP models [Sommerschield et al. 2023]; [Assael et al. 2022]; [Bamman and Burns 2020]; [Riemenschneider and Frank 2023]; [Hellwig 2015]; [Nehrdich, Hellwig, and Keutzer 2024]; [Nehrdich and Keutzer 2026]. Recent work on Ancient Chinese and Ancient Greek translation suggests that modern LLM competence on contemporary lan-guages does not automatically transfer to classical texts, and that rare technical terminology can predict catastrophic failure [Zhang and Li 2023]; [Zainaldin et al. 2026]. This study contributes to that line of work by focusing not merely on average translation quality, but on the rate, severity, and typology of errors that matter for scholarly use. The project also belongs to Buddhist digital humanities. Recent work in this area has shown how language-model-based workflows can support distant reading of canonical Buddhist corpora when paired with domain interpretation, as in Bingenheimer, Brody, and Nichols’s topic-modeling study of Chinese Buddhist texts [Bingenheimer, Brody, and Nichols 2025]. The present study applies a similar principle to translation evaluation: computational methods are used to prioritize and structure attention, while interpretation remains anchored in philological judgment. Classical-language translation is also not only a low-resource problem. It is a philological evaluation and a philosophical interpretation problem. The target output must be judged against grammar, inherited commentary and terminology, genre conventions, and the range of defensible translator choices. For Bud-dhist canonical translation, a rendering that is fluent and semantically adjacent may still be unacceptable if it reverses agency, erases a doctrinal distinction, mishandles a stock formula, or turns an elliptical repe-tition into an invented assertion. Buddhist technical vocabulary is especially sensitive because apparently small lexical choices can locate a passage within a wider conceptual network [Buswell and Lopez 2014]. This is why the present study combines multi-reference semantic triage with severity-based adjudication rather than relying on a single metric family. 

# 3 Methods 

3.1 Dataset 

The source corpus was drawn from Sutta Piṭaka texts of the Pali Canon in the Aṅguttara, Majjhima, and Saṃyutta Nikāyas for which overlapping English translations by three reference translators were publicly available online under licenses or terms permitting scholarly use: SuttaCentral for Sujato and Bodhi [SuttaCentral n.d.] and dhammatalks.org for Thanissaro [Thanissaro Bhikkhu n.d.]. This initial corpus contained 380 SuttaCentral source files (texts): 100 Aṅguttara Nikāya (AN) files, 39 Majjhima Nikāya (MN) files, and 241 Saṃyutta Nikāya (SN) files. In SuttaCentral’s data model, a segment is the smallest addressable text unit, identified by a stable JSON key such as mn2:1.1 ; a passage is a group of segments sharing the same structural prefix, such as mn2:1 . These files contained 22,384 SuttaCentral segments grouped into 4,003 passage candidates. Passages are used here as the unit for both translation and evaluation because they are more semantically complete and meaningful than individual segments. Sujato’s Pali and English texts were already available in segmented JavaScript Object Notation (JSON) format with stable segment identifiers from SuttaCentral’s GitHub repository. Thanissaro’s and Bodhi’s independently segmented translations were aligned to those identifiers with GPT-5-mini using structured JSON output: for each batch of 15-30 segments, the model received the Pali source, Sujato’s English translation as a segmentation guide, and the target translator’s English text, then extracted the minimal corresponding target-translation span for each segment. The alignment system prompt is reproduced in Appendix C. Each extracted Thanissaro and Bodhi segment was then verified against the source files by exact matching, normalized matching, ellipsis-expansion matching, or cross-reference matching. Filtering was designed to remove incomplete, anomalously aligned, extremely short, or highly repetitive candidates before model evaluation, so that later outlier analysis would reflect translation behavior rather 4than corpus-construction artifacts. The first seven passage-level filters were non-exclusive and reduced 4,003 candidates to 1,874 passages. The final near-duplicate removal pass removed 174 additional passages and produced the final 1,700-passage test set. The final test set contains 1,700 Pali passages: 738 SN passages, 553 MN passages, and 409 AN passages, spanning 8,389 source segments. The Pali source side contains 645,876 characters, 70,846 whitespace-delimited words, and approximately 345,000 model tokens using the cl100k_base encoding. As a rough scale comparison, this is on the order of 200-250 printed A4 pages at 12-point type with 1.5 line spacing, depending on margins and font. Median source length is 34 whitespace-delimited Pali words, with a mean of 41.7. Each item also contains the corresponding English reference translations by Bhikkhu Sujato, Bhikkhu Thanissaro, and Bhikkhu Bodhi [SuttaCentral n.d.]; [Thanissaro Bhikkhu n.d.]; [Bodhi 2000]; [Bodhi 2012]; [Ñāṇamoli and Bodhi 1995]. The three-reference design is central to the method. Because Pali Buddhist texts admit legitimate varia-tion in register, terminology, compression, and interpretation, no single reference translation is treated as definitive. Instead, the spread among the three human translations defines a local reference envelope against which candidate translations can be compared. SuttaCentral also functions as digital humanities infrastructure: its structured texts, aligned translations, segment identifiers, and public reading/search environment make passage-level computational comparison possible.

3.3 Embedding Drift and Outlier Triage 

Each model produces an English candidate translation, and this English output is compared with the three English human reference translations. The candidate translation and each reference translation are embedded with Qwen3 Embedding 8B [Zhang et al. 2025], selected because its technical report re-ports strong performance on broad text-embedding benchmarks, including the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB) [Muennighoff et al. 2023]; [Zhang et al. 2025]. Pali source text is not embedded for the drift calculation. For each passage, we compute similarity from the candidate to each reference and to the reference centroid. The main triage statistic is normalized drift, calculated in three steps. First, the three English reference translations for a passage are embedded and averaged to form a reference centroid. Second, the Eu-clidean distance between the candidate translation embedding and this centroid is computed. Third, that candidate-to-centroid distance is divided by the average Euclidean distance between each human refer-ence embedding and the same centroid. Thus, a normalized drift value of 1.0 means that the candidate is about as far from the center of the reference set as the references are from one another on average; values above 1.0 indicate increasing distance outside the local reference envelope. Normalized drift is interpreted as a triage score, not an error label. A high-drift translation is outside the local human-reference envelope and therefore deserves adjudication, but it may still be a valid translation variation. Conversely, a low-drift translation is not guaranteed to be error-free, though (as the validation results below indicate) the risk of a major error is substantially lower in the low-drift range. Consequently, passages where normalized drift exceeded 1.5 were selected for LLM adjudication. Results are stratified into five drift bands: 0.0-1.0 , 1.0-1.5 , 1.5-2.0 , 2.0-3.0 , and >3.0 .

The LLM judges use five top-level labels as described in Table 4.

3.6 Author-Adjudicated Validation Set 

To calibrate the relation between embedding drift and translation error, and to measure how well the LLM judges reproduce a human adjudicator’s labels, we created a 300-item author-adjudicated validation set where both the LLM judge panel and one of the authors adjudicated passages. This validation sample was stratified by normalized drift so that it included both low-drift translations, where major errors were expected to be uncommon, and high-drift translations, where the audit method expected errors to be enriched. The sample contains 100 items from the 0.0-1.0 band, 100 from 1.0-1.5 , 50 from 1.5-2.0 , and 50 from >2.0 . The author adjudicator has formal training in Pali, but this validation subset is not described as independent expert validation. 

3.7 Main LLM Adjudication Subset 

For the primary model-level analysis, all translation instances with normalized_drift > 1.5 were selected and sent to the three-judge panel. This subset contains 1,203 translation instances: 224 from GPT-5.5, 302 from Claude Sonnet 4.6, 257 from Gemini 3.1 Pro, and 420 from Grok 4.3. The result-set analysis uses the majority-panel label as the primary adjudicated outcome. Error-category analysis is restricted to instances labeled MINOR_ERROR or MAJOR_ERROR by the majority panel. If judges agreed that an item was an error but assigned different error categories, we used the category chosen by the largest number of error-labeling judges. Error rates reported for the LLM-adjudicated result set refer only to this high-drift subset ( normalized_drift > 1.5 ). The author-validation sample is used separately to interpret the likely error burden below the full-adjudication threshold. Where confidence intervals are reported, they are two-sided 95% Wilson score intervals for binomial proportions [Wilson 1927]. 

3.8 Automatic metrics 

For descriptive comparison across translator models, we also report automatic metrics over the full 1,700-passage test set. sim_best is the mean highest embedding similarity between a candidate translation and any of the three English references. COMET average is the mean COMET score against the three references, included as a learned MT metric from the COMET and WMT metrics tradition [Rei et al. 82020]; [Rei et al. 2022]; [Freitag et al. 2022]. Length ratio is the candidate translation length divided by the average reference length for the same passage, averaged across passages. These metrics are used as descriptive context and outlier-triage support, not as substitutes for adjudicated error labels.

Although the adjudication schema allowed UNSURE_CONTEXT_NEEDED and REFERENCE_OR_ALIGNMENT_PROBLEM , these labels had zero author-label counts and zero majority-panel counts in the validation set, so the confusion matrix reduces to the three substantive translation-quality labels. The corresponding three-class Cohen’s kappa values are 0.424 for DeepSeek/Kimi, 0.573 for DeepSeek/Qwen, and 0.443 for Kimi/Qwen. 4.8 Shared and Model-Specific Failures 

Within the high-drift judged set, major errors were mostly model-specific rather than shared by all trans-lators on the same Pali passage. Among high-drift judged items, 150 unique Pali passages had at least one model with a major error. Of these, 132 passages had a major error in only one judged translator, 17 in two judged translators, and 1 in three judged translators. No passage had a high-drift judged major error in all four translator models. For any error, including minor errors, overlap was broader but still far from universal. There were 258 unique Pali passages with at least one high-drift model error: 195 involved one translator, 47 involved two translators, 15 involved three translators, and 1 involved all four translators. 4.9 Consistency Between Validation and Full Result Set 

The author-validation and full-result-set analyses agree on the main qualitative finding: higher drift is associated with a higher probability of major error, especially above 2.0 . In the author validation set, major errors rose from 2.0% in 1.5-2.0 to 22.0% above 2.0 . In the full judge-adjudicated result set, major errors rose from 7.9% in 1.5-2.0 to 23.5% above 2.0 when 2.0-3.0 and >3.0 are combined. The higher panel-labeled major-error rate in the full 1.5-2.0 band is partly explained by translator composition. The validation sample is approximately balanced by translator in each band, whereas the full 1.5-2.0 result set contains 219 Grok 4.3 instances and Grok has the highest major-error rate in that band (12.8%). By contrast, GPT-5.5’s full 1.5-2.0 panel-major rate is 2.1%, close to the author-validation rate for the band. The robust conclusion is therefore not that 1.5-2.0 is clean, but that error composition in this band is model-dependent, while >2.0 , and especially >3.0 , is strongly enriched for major translation failures. 

# 5 Discussion 

This study separates three claims that are often collapsed in automatic translation evaluation. First, seman-tic distance from human references is not the same as error. Most 1.5-2.0 outliers, and a substantial fraction of even higher-drift outliers, were judged to be valid translation variations. This supports the multi-reference premise that legitimate translation variation must be modeled rather than treated as met-ric noise [Fomicheva, Specia, and Guzmán 2020]; [Wu, Wieting, and Smith 2024]. Second, semantic distance is still useful as a triage signal. Major errors cluster strongly in the upper tail, especially above 

> 3.0

. Third, minor errors behave differently from major errors. They appear even at low and moderate drift and are harder for LLM judges to distinguish consistently from valid variation. The practical implication is that multi-reference embedding outliers are best used as an audit queue, not as an automatic error label. This is consistent with the broader MT evaluation literature: automatic metrics are valuable, but their strongest role is often comparative or diagnostic rather than determinative for individual translation correctness [Post 2018]; [Mathur et al. 2020]; [Freitag et al. 2022]. For a classical language, this is useful, because it avoids the implausible claim that one English translation is the only correct rendering of a Pali passage, while still finding the region of the output distribution where serious translation failures are concentrated. The model comparison also shows why adjudication is necessary. Gemini 3.1 Pro had the strongest 15 automatic metrics, while GPT-5.5 had the lowest point estimate for adjudicated high-drift major-error burden. The confidence intervals for GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.1 Pro, and Claude Sonnet 4.6 overlap, so these three should not be treated as cleanly rank-ordered by major-error rate. Grok 4.3, by contrast, had both weaker automatic metrics and a much higher concentration of major errors in the high-drift tail. These findings would be blurred if the analysis reported only average embedding similarity, COMET, or raw outlier counts. The positive result should not be missed. For the strongest models, most translations either stayed below the audit threshold or, when flagged, were often still judged defensible. This supports a practical use case for flagship LLMs as generators of first-draft translations, parallel renderings, or review candidates in a human scholarly workflow. Their value is speed, coverage, and the ability to produce plausible English renderings at scale. Their limitation is authority: they do not possess the lived practice context, tradition-specific accountability, or interpretive responsibility that shape a translator’s final choices, and they still occasionally make major errors that a fluent surface can conceal. For digital humanities (DH) and Buddhist-studies readers, the more important result is not that one model ranks ahead of another. It is that failures have different scholarly consequences. A mistranslated agent, reversed polarity, or doctrinally misleading term changes what a reader can responsibly infer from a canonical passage. The leaderboard is therefore best understood as an audit instrument: it identifies which systems produce more material requiring review and what kinds of review are needed. Transla-tors, editors, teachers, students, and digital text projects could use such a workflow to prioritize human correction, not to certify machine translation as a replacement for philological work. The error taxonomy points to recurring risk areas in Pali-to-English LLM translation and aligns with prior work showing that severe MT failures require more than average quality scores to detect [Raunak, Menezes, and Junczys-Dowmunt 2021]; [Guerreiro et al. 2023b]; [Dale et al. 2023]. Omission and truncation were the most common major failures, suggesting that completeness is a central audit target. Doctrinal terms were frequent across both minor and major errors, which is expected in Buddhist canoni-cal translation: small lexical choices can have significant interpretive consequences. Findings on Ancient Greek translation are relevant here: rare technical terminology can be a strong predictor of catastrophic failure even when surrounding prose is translated well [Zainaldin et al. 2026]. Agent, role, negation, and logical-relation errors were less frequent but often severe because they directly change who acts, what is affirmed, or how clauses relate. The shared-failure analysis suggests that high-drift major errors do not simply occur on the same hard passages for every model. Most observed high-drift major errors were single-model events. This matters for interpretation: a model-level error survey can reveal complementary failure modes that disappear in average score tables. Because below-threshold companion translations were not judged, this should be read as a result about drift-flagged major errors rather than definitive proof that the other models translated those passages correctly. The broader methodological contribution is a reusable audit design for classical-to-modern translation workflows. Where multiple human references exist, reference spread can define a local envelope of ac-ceptable variation; embedding drift can prioritize review; a small human-adjudicated validation set can calibrate the relation between drift and error; and an independent LLM judge panel can scale adjudication to the high-risk tail. The output is not just a leaderboard but a map of error burden, severity, and failure modes. This design is especially appropriate for settings where exhaustive expert review is too costly but unvalidated automatic scoring would be too blunt for scholarly use. At the same time, the workflow makes a critical dependency visible. This study uses proprietary flag-ship models both as translation systems and as adjudication aids, while measuring them against human translations embedded in Buddhist textual traditions and contemporary digital infrastructures. That ar-rangement should not be mistaken for transferring textual authority to the models. The LLM judges operationalize a constrained task: compare a candidate with Pali and three references under a declared 16 error taxonomy. They do not settle the meaning of a passage, adjudicate doctrinal interpretation, or replace the translator’s responsibility to make situated choices for a particular audience. The value of the workflow is precisely that it keeps model output subordinate to human references, declared criteria, validation data, and inspectable examples. Finally, AI translation of philosophical and religious texts is also ethically sensitive. Wider access is valuable, but accessibility should not be treated as separate from the communities, practitioners, transla-tors, and scholars for whom these texts are living or authoritative traditions. Any practical deployment of AI-assisted translation in the religious domain should therefore include consultation with relevant com-munities and domain experts, especially when translations are intended for public religious, educational, or practical use. 

# 6 Limitations 

The main limitation is that the validation set is author-adjudicated rather than externally expert-adjudicated. The author adjudicator has formal training in Pali, but the labels are not an independent expert gold standard and do not include inter-rater reliability. This limitation is partly structural: because Pali is a low-resource philological language, it was not feasible for the authors to assemble a panel of validators who were both expert in Pali and sufficiently fluent in English translation judgment for this study. A future study could add multiple adjudicators to estimate intra-rater stability. The study fully adjudicates only translations above normalized_drift > 1.5 . The validation set shows that minor errors also occur below this threshold, so total minor-error rates are likely underesti-mated by direct high-drift counts. Major errors below 1.5 were uncommon in the validation set, but not impossible. The analysis therefore does not treat below-threshold translations as error-free. All translation is passage-level. Pali passages frequently contain ellipsis, repeated formulas, or depen-dencies on wider discourse context. This design is intentional and follows standard machine-translation evaluation practice, but it should be kept in mind when interpreting the results: the human reference translations were produced with access to broader textual context, whereas the LLMs translated only one passage at a time. For API efficiency, passages were batched by token count, but these batches were not designed to provide reliable discourse context because filtering and deduplication removed intervening passages and each passage was still requested as an independent JSON item. The embedding triage model is Qwen3 Embedding. Qwen-family translator outputs were not included in the translator panel to avoid shared-family concerns. Even so, embedding choice can affect drift scores and should be treated as part of the measurement system and a design choice, rather than as a neutral oracle. 

# 7 Conclusion 

This study shows that flagship LLMs can be genuinely useful for Pali-to-English translation, especially as draft generators within a human-led workflow. It also shows why that usefulness must be paired with audit. Multi-reference semantic outliers are a strong starting point for review, but not a substitute for error adjudication. High normalized drift is a signal for major translation failures, especially in the extreme tail, while many moderate outliers remained valid variations and minor errors appeared across the drift spectrum. The strongest practical result is therefore methodological: reference-aware drift can prioritize review, but severity-labeled adjudication is needed to estimate real error burden. Across four flagship translator models, serious failures were not evenly distributed. Grok 4.3 had the largest high-drift tail and the highest adjudicated major-error rate, while GPT-5.5, Claude Sonnet 4.6, 17 and Gemini 3.1 Pro were closer and had overlapping confidence intervals for high-drift major errors. The most important error types were omission or truncation, doctrinal terminology, agent or role errors, additions or hallucinations, and negation or polarity errors. These are precisely the failures most likely to mislead readers of classical or doctrinal texts. The proposed framework is intended to be reusable beyond Pali when multiple human references are available: define the local reference envelope, use embedding drift as a triage signal, calibrate with human adjudication, and scale review with an independent judge panel. The result is not only a model comparison, but an error survey that identifies where LLM translations fail, how severely they fail, and whether failures are shared across models or model-specific. The immediate method introduced in this study requires existing reference translations, but its practical implication extends to texts that do not yet have translations in the target language. A reference-rich subset can be used as an audit bed to estimate which models are reliable enough for draft generation, which error types require human review, and which warning signs should trigger retranslation or escalation to a specialist. The resulting workflow does not authorize unsupervised publication of AI translations. It supports a more modest and useful practice: using LLMs to produce first drafts of untranslated material while understanding error modes, applying empirically grounded checks, targeted review priorities, and human judgment before any translation is treated as trustworthy.

# D Author Validation Instructions 

The author validation subset uses the same top-level label schema as the LLM judges, but with a lighter annotation form to keep the human workload realistic. The author adjudicator sees the Pali source, three references, candidate translation, normalized drift score, and metric metadata, but does not see candidate 30 model identity or LLM judge labels. Author adjudication fields: 

> {"label": "...", "error_category": "...", "severity": "...", "confidence": "...", "notes": "" }

Required fields: • label : required for every item. • error_category : required for MINOR_ERROR and MAJOR_ERROR ; use NONE otherwise. • severity : required for every item. • confidence : recommended for every item. • notes : optional; use when helpful, especially for errors, uncertainty, or possible reference/alignment problems. Author label guidance: • Use VALID_VARIATION when the candidate is defensible even if it differs from all references. • Use MINOR_ERROR for a real, correction-worthy translation problem that does not substantially change the passage’s main meaning. The core agents, actions, negation, doctrinal point, logical relation, and practical implication remain recoverable. • Use MAJOR_ERROR only when the candidate materially changes, obscures, or loses the passage’s meaning. A reader relying only on the candidate translation would be materially misled. • Use REFERENCE_OR_ALIGNMENT_PROBLEM when the item itself appears problematic. • Use UNSURE_CONTEXT_NEEDED when wider sutta context is necessary for a fair decision. • When in doubt between MINOR_ERROR and MAJOR_ERROR , prefer MINOR_ERROR unless the expected misunderstanding is material. The author should make a best effort to ground each decision in the Pali source and the three references, while acknowledging uncertainty where relevant.
