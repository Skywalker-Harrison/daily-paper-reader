## How Far Can Machine Translation Quality Take You? Extrinsic Discourse Evaluation in Goal-Oriented Setups 

Wafaa Mohammed Kata Naszadi Vlad Niculae 

Language Technology Lab, University of Amsterdam 

{w.m.a.mohammed,k.naszadi,v.niculae}@uva.nl 

Abstract 

> Existing machine translation (MT) metrics and discourse-focused evaluations primarily assess translation quality intrinsically, without mea-suring the downstream consequences of transla-tion errors. In this work, we focus on extrinsic discourse evaluation of machine translation un-der two distinct regimes: static and interactive. Under the static regime, we propose an entity counting task as a probe of referential consis-tency in discourse. We show that high intrin-sic MT quality does not reliably predict down-stream discourse success and strong MT sys-tems still produce referential inconsistencies. For the interactive regime, we study the goal-oriented multi-agent Welfare Diplomacy game as a probe of long-horizon communication and coordination. We find that interaction-specific translation failures impact downstream coordi-nation. Our results highlight goal-oriented envi-ronments as a viable framework for discourse-sensitive extrinsic MT evaluation. 1

1 Introduction 

Machine Translation evaluation has traditionally focused on intrinsically measuring the quality of generated translations in isolation (Freitag et al., 2024). However, MT systems are often deployed as intermediate components in larger pipelines such as business workflows or interpersonal communi-cation, where their utility is determined by down-stream task performance. Human-computer inter-action research has studied the impact of machine translation when it mediates interpersonal commu-nication (Zhang et al., 2022b), highlighting in par-ticular the importance of discourse (Yamashita and Ishida, 2006). Many discourse errors preserve lo-cal plausibility while altering cross-sentence in-terpretation, referential consistency, or interaction dynamics, making them difficult to capture with in-trinsic metrics alone. However, discourse-focused

MT evaluation has largely remained intrinsic, re-lying on targeted test suites and discourse-specific automatic metrics (Müller et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Mohammed et al., 2024). Prior work has shown that standard MT metrics often correlate weakly with downstream perfor-mance (Moghe et al., 2023), yet the downstream consequences of discourse-related mistranslations remain insufficiently understood. In this work, we study extrinsic discourse evalu-ation of MT through goal-oriented environments, where translation quality is measured by its effect on downstream interpretation and coordination. We examine two complementary settings: a static set-ting that isolates localized discourse interpretation failures, and an interactive regime that captures how failures propagate through sequential commu-nication and coordination. Firstly, we design a con-trolled static setting of counting person entities in a text. This setting isolates referential consistency as the primary phenomenon of interest, which can be directly impacted by machine translation errors in grammatical correctness, anaphora resolution, and named entity translation, and allows us to directly measure how specific translation errors affect task success. Despite its simplicity, this setup captures core capabilities required in high-stakes applica-tions such as legal interpretation, contractual liabil-ity, and threat assessment. Secondly, we study dis-course in a dynamic multi-agent multi-turn commu-nication setup. We utilize the strategic game Diplo-macy, specifically the Welfare Diplomacy variant proposed by Mukobi et al. (2023). This environ-ment introduces long-horizon, interactive, and goal-driven communication, where translation errors can propagate, compound, and potentially influ-ence outcomes. Multi-agent communication games have grown increasingly sophisticated in recent years, with agent interactions covering non-trivial discourse phenomena such as complex anaphora resolution, conversational repair, and pragmatic im-1plicature (Abdelnabi et al., 2023; Horst et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2024). These environments enable closed-loop evaluation for discourse in machine translation: the downstream impact of translation quality can be assessed automatically through game outcomes, and tracked across multiple conversa-tional turns. Using MT quality estimation metrics and qualita-tive evaluation, we uncover translation aspects that influence downstream outcomes. Our contributions can be summarized in the following: • We propose a framework for extrinsic dis-course evaluation in MT based on two comple-mentary goal-oriented regimes: a controlled static interpretation task and an interactive multi-agent communication environment. • We introduce an entity counting setup that iso-lates discourse-level translation errors involv-ing referential inconsistencies and coreference failure modes. • We study multilingual communication in Diplomacy and show that intrinsic MT qual-ity rankings transfer weakly to downstream coordination outcomes. • Among the models evaluated, those with the highest COMETQE scores (eurollm 22B and ayaexpanse 8B) exhibit persistent discourse-level errors in coreference and coordination that impact downstream performance. • We discuss the current state of MT adoption and outline directions and recommendations for moving toward more task-based evaluation frameworks. 

2 Entity Counting 

The objective of this task is to determine the num-ber of distinct individuals referenced in a given text. Although the task is intentionally controlled and structurally simple, it captures capabilities that are critical in high-stakes domains such as legal interpretation, contractual liability, and threat as-sessment. Real-world failures in this task are docu-mented in news reports by Bhuiyan (2023); Deck (2023), which describe asylum applications being rejected when the single person pronoun “I” is mis-translated as “we”, incorrectly implying that the ap-plication is for more than one person. Unlike open-ended evaluations, the entity counting task provides a tightly controlled probe where downstream fail-ure can be directly attributed to discourse-level translation distortions. 

2.1 Experimental Details 2.1.1 Data 

We build on the English dataset introduced by Gau-tam et al. (2024), originally designed to evaluate pronoun fidelity, i.e., the ability to correctly reuse pronouns given an entity-pronoun pair introduced in context. All instances are unambiguous, with each pronoun having a single identifiable referent. To construct a controlled evaluation setting, we subsample the dataset according to the following criteria: • Each instance consists of a simple narrative involving a single individual, with a focus on pronominal reference. • We consider masculine (he/him/his) and fem-inine (she/her/her) third-person singular pro-nouns across nominative, accusative, and de-pendent possessive cases. 2

• No additional entities or distractors are intro-duced to the text. The resulting subset comprises 360 instances. An example of the data is presented in Table 1. Our preliminary experiments showed that large language models (LLMs) fail to produce consis-tent entity counts, exhibiting sensitivity to prompt phrasing and whether an explanation is requested. This is in line with reported unreliability of LLMs as judges (Wang et al., 2024a; Chehbouni et al., 2026), which undermines their usability for the task. Consequently, we rely on human annotation. Native speakers of the target languages, recruited as volunteers from our research group, are asked to annotate the dataset. Detailed annotation guide-lines are in §A.

3 Multi-Agent Communication 

We study the game Diplomacy , specifically the 

Welfare Diplomacy (WD) variant proposed by Mukobi et al. (2023), in which players must bal-ance military expansion with domestic welfare. In standard Diplomacy (SD), seven players control military units with the objective of capturing supply centers (SCs). The game proceeds in yearly cycles consisting of Spring, Fall, and Winter phases. Dur-ing Spring and Fall, players issue orders to their units. In Winter, players may build new units if their SC count has increased over the preceding year, or must disband units if it has decreased. The game ends when a player controls 18 SCs. In the “full-press” SD setup, players can exchange private messages prior to issuing orders in each phase. WD introduces three key modifications to this setup: (1) after each Winter phase, players receive Welfare Points (WPs) equal to the difference between the number of SCs they control and their number of units on the board; (2) the game terminates after a fixed number of years instead of a victory thresh-old; and (3) each player aims to maximize their own WPs, meaning there is no single winner. We experiment on WD under the following constraints: the game lasts four years, with three phases per year, and five communication rounds per phase. During communication rounds, players exchange messages but do not explicitly reveal order-level details. Orders are submitted only at the end of each phase and are executed immediately. 

3.1 Experimental Details 3.1.1 Communication setups 

We consider four communication settings: • Monolingual communication: All players communicate in a single language (English or one of the target languages). This serves as a baseline to verify that LLM agents are compe-tent in each language, ensuring that observed errors in the multilingual setting stem from translation rather than difference of game per-formance based on language. • Multilingual communication: Players are partitioned into two groups, each using a different language. Each player only reads and writes messages in its assigned language. Communication between groups is mediated by a translation system, which translates mes-sages across languages (see Figure 2). • No-press baseline: No messages are ex-changed throughout the game and players only issue orders. This enables studying communi-cation’s effect on the game’s outcome. • Random communication baseline: Players exchange random messages (noise). This en-ables comparing meaningful communication to non-informative interaction. We use the same random policy setup as that of Mukobi et al. (2023). To facilitate reproducability, we provide examples of different game components in §E, including sys-tem and user prompts, summarization and trans-lation prompts, as well as of the random message baseline.

3.1.3 Evaluation 

Similar to the entity counting task, we evaluate translation quality using reference-free COMETQE metric. For game performance, we report four measures: mean welfare : average welfare points across all powers at the end of the game, capturing overall prosperity, Nash welfare : geometric mean of yearly welfare per power, reflecting fairness and cooperative balance, miscoordination : num-ber of invalid or failed orders including bounced, void, and no convoy actions, and average conflicts :mean number of contested tiles where multiple units attempt to move or hold simultaneously.

Discourse phenomena and their correlation with overall translation and game performance. 

While a comprehensive manual analysis of all message-level phenomena is infeasible at scale, we use several automatically identifiable proxies to study error patterns in the messages exchanged dur-ing the game. Examples of each phenomenon are in §C: • Length mismatch: Discrepancy in infor-mational density between source and target (length ratio in words > 3.0 or < 0.25). • Assistant preface: Presence of meta-communicative or procedural framing in the target (“sure, here is the translation”, “here is the translation”). • Digit mismatch: Inconsistencies in 4-digit numerical strings (e.g., 1901) between source and target, reflecting failures in preserving referential anchors. • Mood change: Source and target differ in the sentence mood (measured by the number of question marks "?"), which impacts negotia-tion dynamics and information elicitation. • Acronym mismatch: Inconsistent rendering of uppercase 2-5 letter acronyms (e.g., map to-kens like ENG, NTH, or game acronyms like WP) between source and target, disrupting shared reference and introducing ambiguity in strategic communication. • Disarmament mistranslation: Cases where 

disarmament is mistranslated as its antonym 

armament , affecting strategic interpretation. • Formality increase: Shifts from informal (e.g., “Let’s stick to the deal”) to formal (e.g., “It is in our mutual interest”) register between original and translated English mes-sages, measured via formality lexicons (§D). This phenomena uniquely affects English-German multilingual setups. We expect these discourse phenomena to vary in their impact on downstream outcomes. 4 Discussion 

MT-mediated interaction has become main-stream. Machine translation increasingly medi-ates everyday communication between speakers of different languages. It is routinely embedded in ex-tended, goal-oriented interactions such as customer support, collaborative work, travel coordination, and social communication (Yamashita and Ishida, 2006; Zhang et al., 2022b; Mohammed et al., 2024), where conversations unfold across multiple turns. In these settings, translation errors can accumu-late over time, distort shared understanding, and affect the interaction outcome. This raises the im-portance of discourse-level phenomena, including coherence, consistency, referential accuracy, and pragmatic alignment, which traditional MT evalua-tion metrics are not designed to capture. Therefore, evaluating MT in interactive, multilingual settings requires moving beyond intrinsic metrics toward frameworks that capture how translation quality in-fluences interaction dynamics and task outcomes. 

Strong MT systems fail at basic referential con-sistency in discourse. Even in a maximally con-trolled setting, strong MT systems struggle with maintaining consistent reference to a single entity across a short text. This is particularly striking, as it establishes a surprisingly weak baseline for discourse-level reliability. While modern MT sys-tems achieve strong performance on evaluation benchmarks, these results suggest that basic forms of contextual consistency are not adequately cap-tured by existing overall quality metrics. This gap is especially concerning in interactive settings, where referential inconsistencies can compound over multiple turns and interfere with shared under-standing between participants. 

LLM communication games for goal-oriented MT evaluation. LLM-based agents have demon-strated strong performance in multi-agent commu-nication games, suggesting that modern language models are capable participants and reliable com-municators in complex interactive settings (Xia et al., 2024). These LLM-to-LLM communication games offer a new path toward automated and goal-grounded evaluation by combining the efficiency of automated evaluation with task-anchored inter-action signals. It allows translation quality to be assessed in terms of its effect on goal achievement rather than isolated linguistic proxies. 

Shifts between intrinsic and extrinsic MT eval-uation. Early MT research prioritized external, application-based evaluation in which translation quality is judged through performance on down-stream tasks carried out by human users in realistic settings (see §5). Over time, evaluation shifted to-ward intrinsic, metric-based approaches, a trend reinforced by the rise of large language models and scalable benchmarking paradigms such as LLM-as-a-judge, which made automatic evaluation more practical and reproducible (Kocmi et al., 2025). As 7discussed by Reiter (2025), this shift is also driven by structural factors in the field: limited exper-tise in impact-oriented evaluation, the benchmark-centric machine learning culture, and the practical difficulty and cost of conducting real deployment studies. Recent work has begun to challenge this paradigm, arguing that effective evaluation should be anchored in the needs of those deploying and using translation systems and integrated into de-velopment pipelines that explicitly account for ex-ternal constraints and objectives, rather than be-ing detached from application contexts (Kayano and Sugawara, 2025; Saphra et al., 2024; Schwartz et al., 2026). 

5 Related Work 

Discourse evaluation in MT. Studying discourse characteristics in MT has been going on since the early works on rule-based and statistical machine translation systems (Hardmeier, 2012). Works in-volve creating discourse-specific datasets (Jiang et al., 2023; Jwalapuram et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2022), developing model architectures to specif-ically target discourse translation (Bawden et al., 2018; Tiedemann and Scherrer, 2017), designing evaluation frameworks and metrics targeting dis-course performance (Tan et al., 2022; Bawden et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2023), and interpreting the role of different parts of the input in discourse translation (Mohammed and Niculae, 2024b,a). 

Multi-agent communication. An increasing number of works propose using community of Large Language Models (LLMs) rather than a sin-gle agent to solve complex tasks. For a compre-hensive, communication-focused review of LLM-based multi-agent systems (LLM-MAS), see (Yan et al., 2025). Most influential to our work are sys-tems that utilize natural language as a core tool for inter-agent communication. When evaluating how machine translation impacts goal achievement, tasks requiring free-form, multi-turn communica-tion are especially compelling, as they naturally in-troduce discourse-related challenges. For instance, (Liang et al., 2024) introduce multi-agent debate to improve arithmetic reasoning, where the mixed incentives of the participants spark rich natural lan-guage exchanges across successive turns. Similarly, (Chen et al., 2025) deliberately introduce informa-tion asymmetry in multi-hop question-answering, which necessitates strategic discourse planning as agents must intentionally coordinate what infor-mation to solicit from one another across turns. Furthermore, multi-agent communities containing more than two agents with distinct roles or personas also encourage highly dynamic and intricate lan-guage use (Wang et al., 2024b; Hong et al., 2024; Park et al., 2023). 

Extrinsic machine translation evaluation. 

Task-based MT evaluation has evolved from early extrinsic frameworks linking translation quality to downstream task performance such as extraction, filtering, and multilingual processing (White and Taylor, 1998; Doyon et al., 1999). Subsequent human-based evaluations measure MT usefulness through information extraction tasks, quizzes, proficiency tests, and comprehension assessments (Voss and Tate, 2006; Berka et al., 2011; Matsuzaki et al., 2015; Jones et al., 2007). More recent work introduces toolkits and automated benchmarks for reading comprehension, cross-lingual information retrieval, and LLM-based QA evaluation (Scarton and Specia, 2016; Sun et al., 2020; Fernandes et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025), alongside application-specific studies in e-commerce, medical systems, toxicity detection, triage, and political classification (Zhang et al., 2022a; Zocca et al., 2025; Bell et al., 2025; Martindale and Carpuat, 2025; Osorio et al., 2025). 

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we investigate the usefulness of ma-chine translation in goal oriented applications. We study discourse characteristics of translation in both static and interactive regimes and examine their impact on the downstream task performance. Our results indicate a weak correlation between translation metrics (COMETQE) and downstream performance in both regimes. We further identify several task-specific discourse phenomena such as coreference resolution, named entity translation, negation, and formality that are not adequately captured by COMETQE, and that influence down-stream coreference and coordination performance. Future research could extend this discourse-centric analysis to a broader range of downstream appli-cations of machine translation. Another promising direction is the development of domain– and task-specific automated evaluation metrics that better capture discourse effectiveness and better correlate with downstream utility. 8Limitations 

• In the discourse analysis in the Welfare Diplo-macy game, we investigate the correlation of the different phenomena to the translation and game performance. We emphasize that our findings are purely correlational and should not be interpreted as evidence of causal re-lationships between specific phenomena and observed performance outcomes. • To isolate the effect of the translation quality, we do not present any explicit task-specific context or instructions to the translation mod-els. Future work could study how to improve translations with task specific instructions. • The entity counting task is subject to annotator bias and implicit assumptions during labeling. In particular, linguistic and cultural conven-tions may influence annotation decisions; for example, in some languages it is traditionally acceptable to use masculine forms when re-ferring to occupational nouns with feminine referents (Alenazy et al., 2024). • We rely on a single metric for machine translation evaluation, namely COMETQE. Although COMETQE is a well established reference-free metric for assessing translation quality, other evaluation metrics (such as met-ricX (Juraska et al., 2023, 2024)) may capture complementary aspects of translation qual-ity and could yield different relative patterns across systems or conditions. 

Ethical Considerations 

As discussed in the paper, machine translation is increasingly deployed in high-stakes settings such as legal communication, asylum procedures, and healthcare access, where translation errors may af-fect users’ ability to make informed decisions or complete critical tasks. Our evaluation framework focuses on discourse-sensitive phenomena relevant to goal-oriented interaction, but does not capture many important aspects of real-world communi-cation, including cultural nuance, sociolinguistic variation, demographic bias, or domain-specific institutional requirements. We believe that mean-ingful evaluation of deployed MT systems requires collaboration with external stakeholders, including professional translators, domain experts, institu-tions, and affected communities.
