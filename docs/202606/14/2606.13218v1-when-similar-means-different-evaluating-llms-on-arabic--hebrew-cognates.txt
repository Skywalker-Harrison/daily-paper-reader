Abstract 

Arabic and Hebrew, as closely related Semitic languages, share a substantial lexicon of true cognates, misleading false friends, and mod-ern loanwords. This overlap poses a chal-lenge for cross-lingual semantic understand-ing in large language models (LLMs). To evaluate this capability, we introduce SemCog Bench, a curated benchmark of 1,858 Arabic– Hebrew word pairs with sentence-level anno-tations for cognate identification and seman-tic disambiguation. We evaluate open-source and commercial LLMs across multiple input representations (raw, diacritized, Romanized, and phonetic) and reveal a critical gap in cross-lingual reasoning. While models achieve high accuracy on true cognates, performance drops sharply on false friends and loanwords, reflect-ing a strong reliance on surface-form similarity. Furthermore, sentence-level context yields only modest improvements, suggesting that contex-tual cues alone are insufficient to overcome mis-leading form-based signals. These findings re-veal a fundamental limitation of current LLMs in resolving cross-lingual form–meaning con-flicts and establish SemCog Bench as a rigorous benchmark for multilingual semantic reasoning. Our code and data are publicly available. 1

Through controlled perturbation experiments, we demonstrate that model errors on false friend pairs are driven primarily by cross-lingual surface similarity rather than by or-thographic complexity or morphological am-biguity. 

2 Background and Related Work 

Arabic–Hebrew Linguistic Background Ara-bic and Hebrew are closely related Semitic lan-guages characterized by rich morphology and or-thographic ambiguity. Both employ root-and-pattern word formation, where words are de-rived by combining a consonantal root, often tri-consonantal, with vocalic patterns, resulting in large and sparse vocabularies. In addition, both languages use optional diacritics to represent short vowels– tashkil in Arabic and nikkud in Hebrew– that are typically omitted in modern text (Habash, 2010; Seddah et al., 2013). As a result, many sur-face forms are ambiguous, posing challenges for computational modeling (Tsarfaty et al., 2020). At the same time, the shared Semitic ances-try of Arabic and Hebrew gives rise to numerous lexical parallels, including words with similar or-thographic or phonological forms. These include true cognates, false friends, and loanwords. Cog-nates are words in different languages that share a common historical origin (Lijewska, 2020; Abu-Rabiah, 2025). Some preserve both surface form and meaning across languages, such as Hebrew 

# ČŇĽ (ild , ‘child’) and Arabic YÀ (wld , ‘boy/child’), and are treated as True Cognates. Others retain or-thographic or phonological similarity while under-going semantic divergence, yielding False Friends (i.e., False Cognates). For example, Hebrew ŊĞŇ (lxm , ‘bread’) and Arabic —mÃ (lHm , ‘meat’) share the Semitic root l-H-m but differ in meaning. We further distinguish these categories from loawords. Although loanwords are generally defined as words borrowed from one language into another (Maha-jna, 2019), we use the term to refer to forms bor-rowed from languages that do not share the relevant inherited Semitic root. For instance, Arabic ˙ÊÖÒÉ 

(swšy , ‘sushi’) and Hebrew ĽŹĚŚ (swšy , ‘sushi’) are orthographically and phonologically similar be-cause both are modern borrowings rather than in-herited Semitic cognates. The close relationship between Arabic and Hebrew therefore creates a challenging setting for lexical reasoning, where highly similar forms may reflect shared inheritance, semantic divergence, or independent borrowing. Distinguishing among these cases requires inte-grating orthographic, phonological, and semantic evidence rather than relying on surface similarity alone. Automatic Cognate Detection Automatic cog-nate detection aims to identify historically related words across languages. Early approaches re-lied primarily on orthographic and phonological similarity, using lexical distance measures and subsequence-based methods (List, 2012; Hauer and Kondrak, 2015). More recent work has incorpo-rated neural architectures and cross-lingual repre-sentations, including Siamese networks and feature-rich models for low-resource language pairs (Kano-jia et al., 2020a,b; Akavarapu et al., 2024). While these methods are effective for identifying histor-ically related forms, cognate detection is funda-mentally different from semantic disambiguation. Determining whether similar-looking words pre-serve the same meaning requires distinguishing true cognates from semantically divergent forms and borrowings, a challenge that cannot be solved through form similarity alone. Recent studies have begun examining this challenge in multilingual LLMs, showing that models often rely heavily on orthographic overlap and struggle to disentangle form from meaning when processing false friends and interlingual homographs (Tanwar et al., 2025; Cahyawijaya et al., 2025). Our work extends this line of research by moving beyond binary cog-nate detection to distinguish between true cognates, false friends, and loanwords, while also evaluating semantic disambiguation in context. 

Arabic–Hebrew Bilingual Modeling Prior work has developed resources and models for Arabic– Hebrew processing, including machine transla-tion datasets, multilingual benchmarks, translitera-tion methods, and cross-script representations (Be-linkov and Glass, 2016; Cettolo, 2016; Zaghouani et al., 2024; Gonzalez et al., 2026; Gadd, 2026). Other studies have explored morphology-aware modeling and probing frameworks for Semitic lan-guages (Gazit et al., 2025; Saeed et al., 2025). De-spite these advances, little is known about how mul-tilingual LLMs reason about lexical relationships across closely related languages. In particular, it remains unclear whether models rely on semantic understanding or on superficial form-based cues when encountering words that are orthographically or phonologically similar. Existing benchmarks do not isolate the effects of shared inheritance, seman-tic divergence, and borrowing on cross-lingual rea-soning. SemCog Bench addresses this gap through controlled evaluation of cognate identification and semantic disambiguation in Arabic and Hebrew. 

3 SemCog Bench 

In this section, we describe the construction of Sem-Cog Bench, a benchmark of Arabic–Hebrew true cognates, false friends, and loanwords. Figure 2 illustrates the three-stage benchmark constrcution pipeline: (1) cognate collection, where candidate word pairs are gathered from linguistic resources; (2) word- and sentence-level annotation, where an-notators verify lexical relations and validate con-textual sentences; and (3) Tasks creation, where validated examples are converted into benchmark instances. 

3.1 Categories of Word Pairs 

Following the linguistic distinctions introduced in §2, we organize word pairs into three cate-gories: true cognates, false friends, and loanwords. True cognates are pairs that exhibit both form and meaning similarity across Arabic and Hebrew. False friends are pairs with similar orthographic or phonological forms but different meanings. Loan-words are similar forms that arise through borrow-ing rather than inheritance from a shared Semitic root. These categories form the basis of our bench-mark and allow us to evaluate whether models can distinguish semantic equivalence from semantic di-vergence and borrowing despite surface-form simi-larity. We collect candidate Arabic–Hebrew word pairs from multiple linguistic resources. For true cog-nates and false friends, we primarily rely on a com-parative Arabic–Hebrew lexicon 4 and the bilingual dictionary of the Palisra project (Habash, 2002, 2023), which contains aligned Arabic and Hebrew vocabulary. 5 For loanwords, we aggregate lexi-cal items from Wiktionary, educational resources, linguistic databases, and prior studies on lexical borrowing in Arabic and Hebrew. Appendix A provides additional details about these resources. Following the initial aggregation, we apply part-of-speech (POS) filtering and deduplication, retain-ing only nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs, as these content words carry the semantic informa-tion required for sentence-level interpretation. The resulting candidate lists are then reviewed by hu-man annotators for validation. This process yields 1,027 true cognates, 474 false friends, and 910 loan-words.

3.2 Word- and Sentence-Level Annotation Word-Level Annotation To ensure dataset qual-ity, we employ two fairly compensated annota-tors with native proficiency in Arabic and Hebrew. One annotator holds an MA in Semitic languages, while the other holds a PhD in linguistics. A pri-mary annotator reviews all candidate word pairs, and a second annotator independently reviews a randomly selected 10% subset to measure inter-annotator agreement (IAA). For true cognates and false friends, they assess the presence of seman-tic overlap between the Arabic and Hebrew words. Pairs with at least one shared core meaning are accepted as true cognates, whereas pairs with com-pletely divergent meanings are classified as false friends. For loanwords, annotators verify that both words originate from the same non-Semitic source language and preserve the same meaning. Annota-tors were also instructed to reject archaic terms and named entities (e.g., person and location names). We retain only lexical items that are commonly used in both Modern Standard Arabic (MSA) and Modern Hebrew. Disagreements between the anno-tators are resolved through discussion until consen-sus is reached. The validated word pairs form the basis of the cognate identification task. 

Sentence-Level Annotation Following word-level validation, we use Gemini-3.1-pro-preview to generate contextual sentences for each validated Arabic–Hebrew word pair. Specifically, the model generates an Arabic sentence containing the Arabic word and a Hebrew sentence containing the cor-responding Hebrew word (the generation prompt is provided in Appendix E). Following word-level validation, the annotators review all generated sen-tences for naturalness. A sentence is considered natural if it is grammatically correct and representa-tive of native-speaker usage. For sentences deemed unnatural due to grammatical errors or implausi-ble contexts, annotators provide revised sentences that preserve the intended meaning and target word without modifying its morphological form. De-tailed annotation guidelines for both the word- and sentence-level annotation tasks are provided in Ap-pendix F. 

Inter-Annotator Agreement Following word-and sentence-level annotation, the final dataset con-tains 858 true cognates, 364 false friends, and 636 loanwords, each paired with validated contex-tual sentences (Table 2). We measure word-level IAA using Gwet’s AC1, which is robust to class imbalance (Wongpakaran et al., 2013; Ohyama, 2021). Gwet’s AC1 was 0.87 for true cognates/-false friends and 0.81 for loanwords, indicating sub-stantial agreement. Most disagreements involved borderline cases between potential loanwords and true cognates, motivating an additional verification round before finalizing the annotations. For com-pleteness, we also report Krippendorff’s α scores of 0.38 and 0.45 for true cognates/false friends and loanwords, respectively. We interpret these val-ues cautiously, as Krippendorff’s α is known to underestimate agreement under highly imbalanced label distributions (Vach and Gerke, 2023), which is relevant in our setting where accept decisions are substantially more frequent than reject decisions. 

3.3 Benchmark Tasks 

SemCog Bench comprises two benchmark tasks: Cognate Identification (CI) and Semantic Dis-ambiguation (SD). Both tasks are formulated as multiple-choice questions (MCQs) designed to evaluate whether models can distinguish surface-form similarity from semantic equivalence. 

Cognate Identification (CI) Given an Arabic– Hebrew word pair (WAr , W He ), the model must determine whether the pair represents a true cog-nate, false friend, or loanword. This task evaluates whether models can distinguish lexical similarity arising from shared meaning, semantic divergence, or borrowing, rather than relying solely on surface-form resemblance. We consider two input settings. In the Word-Only setting, the model receives only the target word pair. In the Contextual setting, the model additionally receives contextual sentences in Arabic and Hebrew, (SAr , S He ), providing con-textual evidence about the meaning of each word. We evaluate both a ternary formulation involving all three categories and a binary formulation re-stricted to true cognates and false friends. The latter removes the confounding effect of loanwords and provides a more direct measure of the model’s ability to resolve form–meaning conflicts. 

Semantic Disambiguation (SD) Given a word pair (WAr , W He ) and their corresponding contex-tual sentences (SAr , S He ), the model must deter-mine which sentence or sentences are semantically correct. Each instance is formulated as a three-way MCQ with the following choices: (A) only SAr is semantically correct; (B) only SHe is semantically correct; and (C) both sentences are correct. For true cognate and loanword pairs, we use the validated sentences collected during sentence-level annotation. Since both sentences employ words with the same meaning, they are semantically valid and the correct answer is therefore (C). Input Representations To assess whether mod-els rely on script-specific orthographic cues, we evaluate four input representations: the original undiacritized text, diacritized text, phonetic tran-scriptions (IPA), and Romanized text. For Arabic, diacritics are obtained using CAMeL Tools (Obeid et al., 2020) and IPA transcriptions are derived from the diacritized forms following the approach of Khalifa et al. (2025) with additional linguistic mapping rules. For Hebrew, nikkud and IPA tran-scriptions are generated using Phonikud (Kolani et al., 2025). Romanized forms for both lan-guages are obtained using Uroman (Hermjakob et al., 2018). These representations help disentan-gle the effects of orthographic, phonological, and semantic information on cross-lingual reasoning.

5 Result & Analysis 

RQ1 : Classification and Disambiguation Ta-ble 1 presents the results for 3-class cognate iden-tification and semantic disambiguation using the undiacritized input representation. Although pro-prietary models generally outperform open-source models, all systems exhibit substantial category-level performance disparities. In CI, many mod-els rely heavily on surface-form similarity, achiev-ing high accuracy on true cognates while strug-gling with loanwords. For example, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct achieves 0.0 accuracy on loanwords despite performing substantially better on true cog-nates. SD further exposes this limitation: sentence context alone is often insufficient to resolve form– meaning conflicts. GPT-4o and Qwen3-8B, for instance, achieve only 35.6% and 6.3% accuracy on false friends, respectively. Overall, these results suggest that lexical relation classification and con-textual semantic reasoning are distinct abilities, and that Arabic–Hebrew false friends remain a major challenge for current LLMs. 

RQ2 : Input Representations Figure 3 shows that the original undiacritized text is generally the most effective input representation. In CI, diacriti-zation has only a modest impact, yielding small gains for some models and slight degradations for others. In contrast, IPA and Romanized representa-tions consistently reduce performance, particularly for open-source models. For example, Qwen2.5-7B loses more than 25% in both accuracy and F 1

under these representations. These results suggest that models rely heavily on script-specific ortho-graphic patterns acquired during pretraining. The same trend is even more pronounced in SD. Alternative representations substantially degrade performance, with GPT-5.4 losing more than 45% in most settings. GPT-4o and Qwen3.6-Plus ex-hibit similar declines, particularly under IPA and Romanized inputs. Although a few models show isolated improvements, these gains are neither large nor consistent. Overall, phonetic and Romanized representations do not improve Arabic–Hebrew lex-ical reasoning. Instead, the results suggest that cur-rent LLMs depend heavily on familiar orthographic forms, with performance deteriorating when these cues are removed.

Qwen2.5-7B exhibits even larger gains in both accuracy and F 1, indicating that context can help distinguish true cognates, false friends, and loanwords. However, context is not universally helpful. 6 Conclusion and Future Work 

We introduced SemCog Bench, a benchmark for evaluating cross-lingual semantic reasoning in LLMs through Arabic–Hebrew true cognates, false friends, and loanwords. Across a diverse set of LLMs, we find that models rely heavily on surface-form similarity when reasoning about lexical rela-tionships across languages. Although performance is generally strong on true cognates, models strug-gle to distinguish false friends and frequently over-generalize cognate relationships to modern loan-words. We also showed that neither sentence-level context nor increased model scale fully resolves these errors, and phonetic or Romanized represen-tations further degrade performance. Future work includes extending the benchmark to additional language pairs and developing meth-ods that better integrate orthographic, phonological, and contextual signals for cross-lingual semantic reasoning. Limitations 

This work has several limitations. First, SemCog Bench focuses on Arabic and Hebrew, and its find-ings may not directly generalize to language pairs with different typological, script, or contact pat-terns. Second, although the dataset covers true cog-nates, false friends, and loanwords, some border-line cases between inherited cognates and deeply integrated borrowings may still involve linguistic judgment. We reduce this ambiguity through expert annotation and additional verification. Third, our evaluation uses controlled MCQ formats, which support systematic comparison but may not fully reflect open-ended generation, translation, or dia-logue settings. Finally, some input representations rely on automatic diacritization, Romanization, and IPA transcription tools, and proprietary model re-sults may vary with future API updates despite our use of fixed snapshots. 

Ethical Considerations 

This work uses linguistic resources and human-validated sentence examples to study Arabic– Hebrew lexical reasoning in LLMs. The dataset does not contain personal, private, or sensitive user information. Annotators have relevant linguistic expertise, and disagreements are handled through verification and discussion. The main considera-tion is to avoid over-interpreting model behavior as reflecting properties of the languages or their speakers. Our analysis is intended to diagnose limi-tations of LLMs, not to evaluate linguistic commu-nities. By identifying errors on false friends and loanwords, this work aims to support more reliable multilingual systems for tasks such as translation, language learning, and cross-lingual information access. We used AI writing assistance within the scope of “Assistance purely with the language of the pa-per” described in the ACL Policy on Publication Ethics.

C Compute and Hyperparameters 

We evaluate the open-source models on two NVIDIA A40 GPUs, each with 48 GB of mem-ory, and access the proprietary commercial models through their official APIs. All experiments with open-source models are conducted using the LM Evaluation Harness framework 6, which provides a consistent evaluation interface and supports repro-ducible comparisons. For open-source model inference, we use the vLLM framework (Kwon et al., 2023) to improve

computational efficiency and memory usage. To ensure reproducibility, we use deterministic decod-ing with temperature t = 0 and limit the max-imum output length to max _token = 20 . D Evaluation Prompts 

This section details the prompts designed for the cognate identification and semantic disambiguation tasks. These prompts incorporate various input for-mats, including undiacritized text, fully diacritized text, Uroman transliteration, and IPA transcription. 

Cognate Identification (Undiac)                              

> You are provided with an Arabic word and a Hebrew word. Your task is to classify the relationship between the words into one of the following categories: - TRUE_COGNATE -FALSE_FRIEND - LOANWORD Provide only one label as the response: TRUE_COGNATE, FALSE_FRIEND, or LOANWORD. Definitions: TRUE_COGNATE: Words that share acommon Semitic root and possess semantically overlapping meanings. At least one core meaning must be present in both languages. FALSE_FRIEND: Words that exhibit similar orthographic or phonological forms but convey completely different meanings, with no meaningful semantic overlap. LOANWORD: Words that are borrowed from one language into another, resulting in similar forms without a shared Semitic root. Arabic: {arabic_word} Hebrew: {hebrew_word} Answer:

5. Common Mistakes to Avoid 

1.  Over -reliance on form  — Similar -looking words may be False Friends if meanings differ 

2.  Ignoring polysemy  — One shared meaning is sufficient for True Cognate 

3.  Translation bias  — Verify meanings in original languages 

4.  Conjugation differences are  expected and  should not be a factor in the annotation decision.
