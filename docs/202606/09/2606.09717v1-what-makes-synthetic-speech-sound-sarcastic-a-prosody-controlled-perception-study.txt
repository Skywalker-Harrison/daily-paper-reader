# What Makes Synthetic Speech Sound Sarcastic? A Prosody-Controlled Perception Study

# Abstract 

Prosody plays a central role in sarcasm perception, yet pre-vious studies have relied on naturally produced speech that lacks fine-grained control over individual acoustic dimensions. As prosodic cues co-vary in natural data, isolating their in-dependent contributions remains challenging. We introduce a controlled framework using neural text-to-speech (TTS) with prompt-based prosodic conditioning to manipulate speech rate, pitch variation, and loudness. An orthogonal stimulus set was constructed to enable causal testing of prosodic cue effects. Hu-man listeners rated sarcasm and naturalness, and their judg-ments were compared with predictions from a foundation model capable of processing audio input. Results show that loudness primarily drives human sarcasm perception, whereas the model assigns greater weight to speech rate, leading to distinct cue-weighting patterns. This study shows how controllable neural TTS enables investigation of prosodic cue weighting in speech perception. 

Index Terms : sarcasm perception, prosody control, synthetic speech, neural TTS 

# 1. Introduction 

Sarcasm is a pervasive form of verbal irony in which speakers convey an intended meaning that contrasts with the literal con-tent of an utterance. Early research on sarcasm perception has primarily relied on behavioral experiments using naturally pro-duced speech, examining how listeners interpret ironic intent under varying contextual and prosodic conditions [1, 2, 3]. In these paradigms, listeners typically judge speaker intent while prosody and context are contrasted, revealing that prosodic cues strongly bias interpretations toward sarcasm. A large body of perceptual work has since demonstrated that listeners are highly sensitive to pitch modulation, speech rate, loudness, and intona-tion when interpreting sarcasm [4, 5, 6]. Even in the absence of rich contextual information, prosody alone can systematically influence sarcastic interpretations, and its interaction with con-text further shapes comprehension [7, 8]. These perceptual find-ings are complemented by acoustic analyses showing that sar-castic speech exhibits exaggerated or atypical prosodic patterns, including altered pitch contours, temporal structure, and vocal intensity [9, 10, 11, 12]. Cross-linguistic work in Mandarin and Cantonese further demonstrates that these prosodic cues gener-alize across languages, with pitch contour and temporal features playing particularly prominent roles [13, 14, 15]. Despite these advances, a methodological challenge re-mains. Most studies rely on naturally produced speech, typi-cally contrasting sarcastic and literal renditions of the same ut-terance [9, 11]. While such manipulations show that prosody as a whole influences sarcasm perception, they do not pro-vide fine-grained control over individual acoustic dimensions. In natural speech, multiple prosodic features, such as pitch range, speaking rate, and amplitude, co-vary and correlate with each other. Consequently, it is difficult to determine whether a specific dimension independently drives sarcastic interpreta-tions or whether observed effects arise from combined acoustic cues. Post hoc acoustic analyses can describe group differences between sarcastic and literal productions but cannot establish which individual prosodic features causally drive perception. Recent advances in neural TTS technology offer a promis-ing solution. Modern speech synthesis systems can generate highly natural-sounding speech while providing explicit con-trol over prosodic attributes such as pitch, timing, and inten-sity. This makes synthetic speech viable as tightly controlled, perceptually valid stimuli for psycholinguistic experiments. In the present study, we leverage these advances by generating prosody-controlled synthetic speech with Qwen3-TTS [16], a multimodal foundation model that supports controllable and natural speech generation. We manipulate three theoretically motivated prosodic dimensions, speech rate, pitch range, and amplitude, while holding lexical content constant. This design allows us to test the individual and combined contributions of prosodic cues to sarcasm perception. Additionally, we comple-ment the human perception experiment with a computational evaluation, assessing how a large-scale model responds to the same prosodically manipulated stimuli. Comparing human and machine perception allows us to examine the extent to which computational systems are sensitive to the same prosodic ma-nipulations as human listeners. This comparison can also pro-vide insight into convergences and divergences in cue weighting between biological and artificial systems. In addition, it high-lights potential divergences in cue weighting between human and computational systems, offering insight into which acous-tic features are most salient for human sarcasm perception. By integrating controlled synthetic speech, human percep-tion experiments, and machine-based perception, this study pro-poses a novel framework for investigating speech perception. Our results show that human listeners rely primarily on loud-ness cues when interpreting sarcasm, with louder realizations receiving significantly higher sarcasm ratings than softer ones. Model-based evaluation suggests a different pattern. While the foundation model exhibited sensitivity to prosodic variation, its predictions were primarily driven by speech rate, assigning higher sarcasm scores to slowed speech. Beyond clarifying the perceptual role of specific prosodic cues, these findings demon-strate the methodological potential of neural TTS as a tool for constructing tightly controlled speech stimuli and conducting speech perception experiments.

# 2. Method 

2.1. Materials 

The linguistic materials were adapted from the stimulus set pro-vided in Bryant and Fox Tree [1], which consists of short En-glish utterances originally used to investigate verbal irony per-ception in spontaneous speech. In their experiments, these sen-tences were tested to be semantically neutral in isolation and can plausibly convey either a sincere or sarcastic interpretation depending on prosodic realization or context. They served as input for neural speech synthesis to systematically manipulate prosodic dimensions. 

2.2. Speech synthesis and stimulus selection 

All stimuli were generated using the publicly released Qwen3-TTS-12Hz-1.7B-CustomVoice model 1 with a single synthetic speaker voice to eliminate inter-speaker variability and uncon-trolled voice quality differences. We systematically manipu-lated three prosodic dimensions in a fully crossed 2 × 2 × 2 fac-torial design: pitch variation (dynamic vs. flat), loudness (loud vs. soft), and speech rate (fast vs. slow). Prosodic manipula-tions were implemented via natural-language prompting to the TTS model with explicit instructions controlling tempo, pitch variation, and intensity. For example, in the slow flat soft con-dition, the model was instructed to “Speak in a very slow, drag-ging tempo. Keep your voice quiet, almost like mumbling to yourself. Crucially, use a completely flat, monotone pitch with absolutely no emotion or intonation changes.” For each utterance and condition, 100 candidate samples were generated using stochastic decoding with different random seeds. To further modulate prosodic variability, sampling tem-perature was adjusted across conditions. Temperature controls the sharpness of the output distribution during autoregressive decoding [17]. Lower temperature values reduced variability in flat conditions, whereas higher values encouraged expres-sive variation in dynamic conditions. This procedure yielded a large candidate pool for each utterance and condition, from which stimuli were selected based on the orthogonality criteria described below. Acoustic features were extracted from all candidates, in-cluding pitch variation (standard deviation of fundamental fre-quency F0), loudness (root-mean-square intensity), and utter-ance duration. To ensure orthogonality across prosodic dimen-sions, we implemented an effect-size–based stimulus selection procedure. For each utterance and condition pair, candidate samples were contrasted along the relevant factorial dimen-sions, and pairwise differences were quantified using Cohen’s 

d [18] to estimate manipulation strength and potential cross-dimensional spillover onto non-target dimensions. Stimuli were selected to maximize effect sizes in the intended dimension while minimizing effect sizes in non-target dimensions. The candidate combination yielding the optimal orthogonality pro-file was retained as the representative stimulus for each utter-ance and condition. This procedure resulted in a final stimulus set exhibiting large effect sizes in the intended dimensions (pitch variation: 

d = 1 .14 ; loudness: d = 0 .81 ; duration: d = 1 .76 ), while effect sizes in non-target dimensions remained close to zero (all 

|d| < 0.25 ). These results confirm effective and independent manipulation of the three prosodic features, ensuring a con-

2.3. Experimental design 

The final stimulus set followed a fully crossed 2×2×2 prosodic design (pitch variation × loudness × duration). Because lexical content was held constant across prosodic conditions, any per-ceptual differences can be attributed solely to prosodic manipu-lation, as shown in Figure 1. Importantly, since prosodic manip-ulations were implemented through natural-language prompting of a generative TTS system, complete isolation of single acous-tic dimensions cannot be guaranteed. Our orthogonality anal-ysis therefore establishes statistical independence among mea-sured prosodic features, but does not preclude subtle variation in unmeasured dimensions. To avoid this, we check important voice quality measures (H1–H2 and HNR parameters) across the eight conditions and no systematic difference is observed (all p > . 05 ). The 192 stimuli (24 utterances × 8 prosodic conditions) were distributed across eight counterbalanced lists. Each participant was assigned to one list and heard three utter-ances per prosodic condition (3 × 8), yielding 24 unique sen-tences per participant. Within a given list, lexical items did not repeat across prosodic conditions. This design ensured balanced exposure across participants while minimizing fatigue and po-tential lexical confounds. For human sarcasm perception, we recruited 66 speakers with native or reported near-native proficiency in English via an anonymized online questionaire. Participants listened to the tar-get utterances and rated perceived sarcasm and naturalness on separate five-point Likert scales (1 = not sarcastic / very artifi-cial, 5 = very sarcastic / very natural). No contextual informa-tion was provided; judgments were based exclusively on vocal prosody in the synthesized speech. For model-based sarcasm perception, we used Qwen3-Omni [19], a foundation model capable of processing audio input. The model was instructed to simulate a participant in a speech perception experiment and to base its judgments on vocal prosody. For each stimulus, the audio waveform was pro-0

To reduce stochastic variability, inference was repeated with six different random seeds, and ratings were averaged across runs. 2.4. Evaluation and statistical analyses 

All statistical analyses were conducted in R. Linear mixed-effects models were fitted using the lmer function from the 

lme4 package [20]. Speech rate (fast vs. slow), pitch contour (dynamic vs. flat), and loudness (soft vs. loud) were treated as fixed effects, with random intercepts for participants and items. Fast speech rate, dynamic pitch variation, and soft loudness were specified as the reference levels for the categorical pre-dictors. Models were estimated using maximum likelihood. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons were conducted using estimated marginal means obtained from the emmeans package [21]. Multiple comparisons were adjusted using the Tukey method. 

# 3. Results 

We first evaluated reliability using intraclass correlation coeffi-cients (ICC) [22] to justify the use of aggregated human and model ratings in subsequent analyses. Inter-rater reliability for human sarcasm ratings was modest at the individual level (ICC (2 ,1) = .15 ) but high after aggregation (ICC (2 ,k ) = .92 ), indicating that group-averaged judgments provide stable esti-mates. For human naturalness ratings, reliability followed a similar pattern, with low individual agreement (ICC (2 ,1) = .04 )and improved reliability after averaging (ICC (2 ,k ) = .76 ). For model predictions, reliability across seeds was moderate for sar-casm (ICC (2 ,1) = .40 ; ICC (2 ,k ) = .80 ) and lower for natural-ness (ICC (2 ,1) = .25 ; ICC (2 ,k ) = .67 ), suggesting that multi-seed aggregation can also yield stable estimates. Given the rel-ative reliability of aggregated ratings, we proceed to analyze the effects of prosodic manipulations on perceived sarcasm and naturalness for humans and models (see Figure 2). 

3.1. Human naturalness evaluation 

We first examined the perceived naturalness associated with dif-ferent prosodic manipulations. Results showed a significant main effect of speech rate ( β = 0 .090 , p = 3 .1 × 10 −6), in-dicating that fast stimuli were perceived as more natural than slow stimuli. Loudness also showed a significant main effect (β = 0 .113 , p = .0006 ), with soft stimuli receiving higher nat-uralness ratings than loud stimuli. Pitch contour did not show a significant main effect ( β = −0.041 , p = .289 ). Importantly, significant interactions were observed between speech rate and pitch contour ( p = .050 ) as well as between speech rate and loudness ( p = .002 ), suggesting that the effect of temporal and intensity cues on perceived naturalness depends on their combi-nation. In contrast, the two-way interaction between pitch con-tour and loudness and the three-way interaction were not sta-tistically significant (both p > . 05 ). Although prosodic exag-geration reduced perceived naturalness to some extent, overall ratings remained very high for all conditions, suggesting that the stimuli were acceptable for sarcasm evaluation. 

3.2. Human sarcasm perception 

We then examined the perceived sarcasm associated with differ-ent prosodic manipulations. Results showed a significant main effect of loudness ( β = 0 .285 , p = .017 ), indicating that loud stimuli received higher sarcasm ratings than soft stimuli. How-ever, neither speech rate ( β = 0 .061 , p = .617 ) nor pitch con-tour ( β = 0 .138 , p = .248 ) showed significant main effects. None of the two-way or three-way interactions reached statisti-cal significance, indicating that the effects of speed, pitch con-tour, and loudness were largely additive and independent in the current setting. To further examine differences among the eight conditions, Tukey-adjusted pairwise comparisons were conducted based on estimated marginal means from the full factorial model. Com-parisons indicated that conditions combining flat pitch con-tour and loud intensity were generally rated as more sarcastic than their soft counterparts. Specifically, the contrast between 

fast flat soft and fast flat loud was significant ( β = −0.244 ,

p = .046 ). Similarly, slow flat soft received significantly lower ratings than slow flat loud (β = −0.414 , p = .013 ). Across rate conditions, fast dynamic soft was rated significantly lower than fast flat loud (β = −0.382 , p = .032 ) and slow flat loud 

(β = −0.494 , p = .002 ). Additional significant contrasts in-cluded differences between slow dynamic soft and slow flat loud 

(β = −0.433 , SE = 0.120, t = −3.61 , p = .008 ), as well as several comparisons involving loud versus soft realizations within the same pitch configuration. All remaining pairwise comparisons did not reach statistical significance after Tukey correction ( p > . 05 ). The pattern of results confirms that loud intensity consistently increases perceived sarcasm ratings, par-ticularly when combined with flat pitch contours. These find-ings align with previous research demonstrating that prosodic exaggeration plays a central role in sarcasm perception [9, 10]. Although pitch contour showed no significant main effect, its marginal significance is consistent with prior claims that into-national flattening contributes to perceived irony [3]. Combined with the naturalness results, we can conclude that prosodic cues that enhance sarcasm perception (e.g., increased loudness or slowed rate) also introduce markedness that listeners interpret as reduced naturalness. Based on post-experiment interviews with a subset of par-ticipants, their qualitative impressions were consistent with the quantitative findings. Subjectively, fast dynamic soft stimuli were described as sounding sincere and urgent, whereas slow flat loud stimuli were often perceived as sarcastic and some-what anger-like. These observations are consistent with the sta-tistical results showing that loud intensity and flat pitch contour increase perceived sarcasm. 

3.3. Machine naturalness evaluation 

For model-based speech perception, we first examined whether the synthesized prosodic manipulations are perceived differ-ently in terms of naturalness by Qwen3-Omni. Results showed a significant main effect of pitch contour ( β = 0 .115 , p < .001 ), indicating that dynamically modulated utterances were rated as more natural than flat-pitch utterances. In contrast, speech rate did not yield a significant main effect ( β = 0 .083 ,

p = .137 ), nor did loudness ( β = 0 .028 , p = .535 ). No sig-nificant two-way or three-way interactions were observed (all 

p > . 26 ), suggesting that the contribution of pitch modulation to perceived naturalness was robust across different combina-tions of speech rate and intensity cues. Although minor nu-merical differences were observed across prosodic conditions, overall naturalness ratings remained high in all cases (means ranging from 3.95 to 4.25), indicating that the acoustic manipu-lations preserved general perceptual acceptability. 

3.4. Machine sarcasm perception 

We further examined whether the synthesized prosodic ma-nipulations influenced the sarcasm perception of models such as Qwen3-Omni. Results showed a significant main effect of speech rate on sarcasm ratings ( β = 0 .313 , p = .009 ), indicat-ing that slow stimuli were rated as more sarcastic than fast stim-uli. No significant main effects were observed for pitch contour (β = 0 .132 , p = .272 ) or loudness ( β = 0 .035 , p = .773 ). None of the two-way or three-way interactions reached statisti-cal significance after multiple comparison correction. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons with Tukey correction were conducted to further examine differences across the eight prosodic conditions. Significant contrasts were primarily ob-served between extreme prosodic configurations. Conditions characterized by slow rate and loud intensity were consistently rated higher than fast-rate or soft-intensity conditions (e.g., 

p < . 01 for slow dynamic loud vs. fast dynamic soft ). In con-trast, comparisons between conditions that differed in only one prosodic dimension but shared similar acoustic profiles were generally non-significant after correction. Overall, the pairwise results suggest that machine predictions are mainly driven by combinations of prosodic extremes rather than isolated feature changes. 

3.5. Human-model cue-weighting patterns 

To directly compare human and model sensitivity to prosodic manipulations, we examined the alignment between human sar-casm ratings and model-predicted sarcasm scores across condi-tions. A Spearman rank correlation analysis showed no align-ment of the ranking of prosodic conditions between humans and the model ( ρ = −0.11 , p = 0 .26 ). Beyond this overall mis-alignment, systematic differences also emerged in how prosodic cues were weighted by the two systems (Table 1). Human listeners acquire sarcasm sen-sitivity through rich socio-pragmatic experience, where inten-sity cues often signal affective exaggeration. # 4. Discussion 

Our results clarify which prosodic dimensions primarily drive sarcasm perception under controlled synthetic manipulation. Across human judgments, loudness emerged as the most robust predictor of perceived sarcasm, especially when combined with flat pitch contours. This pattern is consistent with the hypothesis that prosodic exaggeration (particularly increased intensity and reduced pitch variability) signals ironic intent, extending prior findings obtained with natural speech [9]. In contrast, speech rate showed a stronger influence in the model’s predictions than in human ratings, indicating a divergence in cue weighting. While the model captured global trends in prosodic exagger-ation, it relied more heavily on temporal variation than human listeners. Pitch contour played a secondary role in both sys-tems. These differences highlight the importance of cue-level analysis when evaluating pragmatic reasoning in state-of-the-art models.
