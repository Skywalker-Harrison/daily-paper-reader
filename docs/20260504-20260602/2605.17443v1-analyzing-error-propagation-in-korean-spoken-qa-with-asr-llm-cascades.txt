# Analyzing Error Propagation in Korean Spoken QA with ASR–LLM Cascades 

Donghyuk Jung ∗ and Youngwon Choi † 

> ∗

Korea Institute of Culture Technology, Republic of Korea E-mail: dhjung081121@gm.gist.ac.kr  

> †

Abstract —We analyze how automatic speech recognition (ASR) errors propagate through ASR–LLM cascades in Korean spoken question answering (SQA), focusing on downstream semantic failures that conventional ASR metrics cannot fully capture. Our analysis shows that the relative downstream degradation caused by ASR errors is consistent across LLMs with differ-ent absolute performance, suggesting that cascade degradation largely tracks ASR-stage information loss. We further identify single-character Korean ASR errors as a distinct semantic-failure channel, where the gold answer becomes entirely absent from the downstream prediction despite only a minimal transcription difference. Finally, an auxiliary comparison shows that a large audio language model outperforms an ASR–LLM pipeline with a matched language backbone in noisy Korean SQA, indicating the potential of direct audio input to mitigate transcript-induced information loss. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used as general-purpose task solvers for applications such as ques-tion answering, information retrieval, and task-oriented dia-logue [1]. As these applications move into user-facing systems, speech has become an important interaction modality, as seen in speech-based chatbots and voice assistants [2], [3]. Acommon implementation is therefore an ASR–LLM cascade, where automatic speech recognition (ASR) first converts the user’s speech into text and the resulting transcript is then passed to an LLM as task input [4]. While this cascade is simple, modular, and easy to deploy, downstream performance becomes dependent on the information preserved in the ASR transcript. Although modern ASR systems have achieved strong tran-scription accuracy, their outputs can still degrade under noisy acoustic conditions [5]. In ASR–LLM cascades, the resulting transcript errors are not merely transcription mistakes, because they can remove or distort the task-relevant information needed by the downstream model [6]. This creates a mismatch between transcription-level error rates and downstream task risk, where an error that appears minor in the transcript can still trigger a complete task failure.

Recent work has begun to evaluate ASR outputs by their downstream impact in LLM-powered applications [6], [7]. However, most existing analyses focus on English, leaving language-specific behavior in ASR–LLM cascades underex-plored. Korean is a particularly important case because many Sino-Korean morphemes are realized as single syllables, so character-level ASR errors can be semantic rather than merely orthographic, limiting the direct generalization of English-centered findings. We address this gap by quantitatively analyzing how ASR errors propagate to downstream QA performance in Korean spoken question answering (SQA). To obtain a controlled range of ASR error levels, we synthesize speech from Korean text questions, apply additive noise to the synthesized speech, transcribe the resulting audio with ASR, and provide the transcripts to Korean-capable instruction-tuned LLMs together with the original context passage. We examine downstream QA degradation, test whether an ASR-aware disclaimer prompt mitigates noisy-input errors, and analyze single-character ASR errors on a per-case basis. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:  

> •

We show that, in Korean SQA, the relative downstream degradation caused by ASR errors is consistent across LLMs with different absolute performance, suggesting that degradation in ASR–LLM cascades is primarily as-sociated with ASR-stage information loss.  

> •

We identify single-character Korean ASR errors as a distinct semantic-failure channel, where the gold answer becomes entirely absent from the downstream prediction despite only a minimal transcription difference.  

> •

We show that a direct-audio language model outperforms an ASR–LLM pipeline with a matched language back-bone in noisy Korean SQA, suggesting that bypassing ASR can reduce transcript-induced information loss. II. E XPERIMENTAL SETUP 

In this section, we describe the experimental setup for analyzing how ASR errors propagate to downstream Korean SQA. We synthesize Korean questions and mix noise at seven signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) levels to induce a broad range of ASR error rates, yielding an observed character error rate

(CER) range of approximately 0.03–0.50. Each noisy question is transcribed by ASR, and the transcript is provided to an LLM as the question, together with the original Korean context passage, for downstream QA evaluation. The full pipeline is illustrated in Fig. 1. 

A. SQA Evaluation Dataset 

We construct a SQA evaluation dataset from KorQuAD v1 [8]. Starting from the validation split of 5,774 samples, we apply three filtering criteria: questions must be between 5 and 100 characters in length, questions containing special characters are removed, and questions containing digits (0– 9) are excluded to avoid ambiguity from digit-to-Hangul con-version during TTS. After filtering, 4,138 candidates remain, from which we select 1,500 samples by stratified sampling to form the final evaluation set. The selected question texts are then synthesized using Google Cloud TTS [9] with the ko-KR-Wavenet-A Korean voice at 16 kHz mono, where each spoken question is paired with its original text context and gold answer. We then construct noisy speech inputs by mixing the synthe-sized questions with noise clips sampled from the noise subset of MUSAN [10]. The sampled noise clips are mixed with the synthesized speech at seven SNR levels from +20 to −10 dB in 5 dB steps, in addition to clean conditions. 

B. Models and Inference Details 

We use Whisper-large-v3 [11] as the ASR system, using the pretrained model without fine-tuning. All speech inputs are decoded in Korean transcription mode using FP16 inference with a batch size of 16. For downstream SQA, we compare four instruction-tuned LLMs: Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct [12], SOLAR-10.7B-Instruct [13], and EXAONE-3.5-32B-Instruct [14]. EXAONE-3.5-32B and Qwen2.5-32B are run with 4-bit AWQ quantization, whereas SOLAR-10.7B

We run ASR inference with PyTorch 2.4 and perform LLM inference using vLLM [15]. We evaluate each LLM under three prompt conditions. In the Oracle condition, the original text question is provided to the LLM together with the context passage, bypassing the ASR stage. In the Disclaimer condition, the same ASR transcript and context passage are used, but the following disclaimer sentence is appended to the QA prompt: “Note: the question text is an ASR output and may contain typos or errors. Please infer the original intent in your answer.” Thus, the Normal and Disclaimer conditions differ only in the presence of this additional prompt sentence. Example prompts for each condition are shown in Fig. 2. Following [16], we apply the same LLM-based answer re-extraction step to all generated responses to obtain concise final answer spans, using EXAONE-3.5-32B. 

C. Metrics 

We use character error rate (CER) instead of word error rate (WER) as the ASR metric, since CER is less affected by Korean word segmentation ambiguity [17]. Downstream QA performance is evaluated using exact match (EM) and F1 score, following the standard KorQuAD evaluation protocol. III. R ESULTS AND ANALYSES 

A. SQA Performance under ASR Corruption 

Table I reports SQA performance in the Oracle and Nor-mal conditions across clean and noisy speech inputs. The induced ASR error level remains below 0.07 down to +5 dB, but rises sharply under severe noise, from 0.26 at −5 dB to 0.50 at −10 dB. Downstream QA performance follows the same degradation pattern. Despite differences in Oracle 

performance, all LLMs show similar baseline-relative F1 re-covery: about 99% at +20 dB, 96% at +5 dB, and 67% 

at −10 dB. These results suggest that low-error transcripts are nearly sufficient for downstream QA, whereas heavily corrupted transcripts impose a bottleneck that is only weakly mitigated by stronger downstream LLMs. 

B. Single-Character ASR Errors as a Korean-Specific Loss Channel 

Korean is particularly sensitive to single-character ASR errors because many Sino-Korean morphemes are realized as single syllables, and phonologically similar syllables can cor-respond to different lexical meanings or syntactic roles. Thus, even a one-character substitution can change the intended question. Table II summarizes the downstream impact of 1,206 cases in which the Whisper-large-v3 reference and hypothesis differ by exactly one character. Among them, 151 cases (12.5%) become semantic failures , where the gold answer is entirely absent from the prediction. With direct audio input, Qwen2.5-Omni-7B recovers 75.5% of these semantic failures. Figure 3 illustrates this pattern with representative cases where a single-character ASR error changes the intended question. 

C. LALM vs. ASR-LLM Pipeline 

Recent studies have raised the possibility that direct au-dio input to LLMs can avoid some of the information loss

introduced by ASR-based cascades [18], [19], [20]. As an auxiliary comparison, we therefore test whether bypassing ASR with a large audio language model (LALM) can reduce the downstream loss observed in ASR–LLM cascades. We use Qwen2.5-Omni-7B-Instruct [21] as the representative LALM and evaluate only its Thinker component, whose Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct language backbone allows an approximately controlled comparison with Whisper-large-v3 + Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. Table III shows that the LALM outperforms the ASR– LLM pipeline at every SNR level in this setting, with gains averaging +0 .058 F1 / +0 .055 EM across SNR conditions and no crossover observed. The gap is already visible at +20 dB, where ASR corruption is relatively small, and becomes larger under severe noise, increasing to a +0 .112 F1 gap at −10 dB. These results suggest the potential of direct audio input as a way to mitigate ASR-transcript information loss in Korean SQA, while remaining preliminary due to the use of a single LALM. D. Effect of Disclaimer Prompting 

Finally, we examine whether the Disclaimer condition miti-gates ASR-induced QA degradation without changing the ASR system or the downstream LLM. We compare the Disclaimer 

condition against the Normal condition, where the two con-ditions use the same ASR transcript and context passage and differ only in an additional sentence informing the LLM that the question is an ASR output. This comparison directly tests whether explicit ASR-error awareness helps recover down-stream QA performance. Table IV reports EM/F1 under the Normal and Disclaimer 

conditions. Contrary to the expectation that an ASR disclaimer would induce more tolerant, intent-oriented reasoning, the disclaimer does not recover noisy QA performance. After iden-tical answer normalization, the disclaimer yields no consistent improvement. Its effect is negligible for the two 32B-class models, mildly negative for Qwen2.5-7B, and clearly detrimen-tal for SOLAR-10.7B. These results indicate that disclaimer prompting is not a reliable remedy for ASR degradation, making ASR robustness the more dependable target for better SQA performance. IV. C ONCLUSION 

We analyzed how ASR errors propagate through ASR–LLM cascades in Korean spoken question answering. Our results show that downstream degradation under ASR corruption is similar across LLMs with different absolute QA performance, suggesting that the main bottleneck in this setting lies in ASR-stage information loss rather than downstream LLM capability. We also identified single-character Korean ASR errors as a semantic-failure channel, where a minimal transcription dif-ference can remove the gold answer from the downstream prediction. In addition, the comparison with a direct-audio lan-guage model suggests that bypassing ASR may help mitigate part of this transcript-induced information loss, while ASR-aware disclaimer prompting does not reliably recover noisy QA performance. This study is limited to Korean SQA based on TTS-synthesized speech, and future work should examine whether the findings generalize to broader spoken language understanding tasksand real-speech conditions.
